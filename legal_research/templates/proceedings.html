Court Proceedings: How Cases Move Through Indian Courts

Understanding the hierarchy and process — from filing to final appeal

Indian Court Hierarchy & Flow of Cases

1

District Court / Trial Court

This is where most cases begin. District courts are the primary courts of original jurisdiction in India.

  • Civil Cases: Suits filed under the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC). Issues summons, records evidence, passes decree.
  • Criminal Cases: FIR → Investigation → Charge sheet → Trial. Magistrate courts handle cases up to 3 years punishment; Sessions courts handle serious offences.
  • Family Courts: Divorce, custody, maintenance disputes under the Family Courts Act.
  • Labour Courts / Tribunals: Industrial disputes, employment claims.
  • Consumer Forums: Consumer complaints at district level (up to Rs. 1 crore).
2

First Appeal — Sessions Court / District Judge

The first level of appeal from lower courts within the district.

  • Appeals from Magistrate orders go to Sessions Court
  • Appeals from Civil Judge (Junior Division) go to District Judge
  • First appeal is a right — the appellate court re-examines facts and law
  • The appeal must be filed within the limitation period (30-90 days depending on case type)
3

High Court

Each state has a High Court. There are 25 High Courts in India. They have both original and appellate jurisdiction.

  • Original Jurisdiction: Writ petitions under Article 226 (fundamental rights violations, habeas corpus, mandamus, etc.)
  • Appellate Jurisdiction: Second appeals from district courts (only on substantial questions of law — Section 100 CPC)
  • Criminal Appeals: Appeals against Sessions Court orders, confirmation of death sentences
  • Revision: Under Section 115 CPC — to correct errors of jurisdiction by lower courts
  • PIL: Public Interest Litigation can be filed directly in the High Court
  • Bail Applications: Regular bail, anticipatory bail (BNSS Section 482)
4

Supreme Court of India

The apex court and the final court of appeal. Located in New Delhi. Its decisions are binding on all courts (Article 141).

  • SLP (Special Leave Petition): Under Article 136, the most common route — SC can grant leave to appeal against any order of any court/tribunal
  • Writ Jurisdiction: Under Article 32 for enforcement of fundamental rights
  • Appeals: Constitutional matters (Article 132), civil matters (Article 133), criminal matters (Article 134)
  • Advisory Jurisdiction: Under Article 143, the President can seek SC's opinion on legal questions
  • Review Petition: Under Article 137, SC can review its own judgment (limited grounds)
  • Curative Petition: Last resort after review is dismissed — to prevent abuse of process
Key Points for Lawyers Moving Between Courts:
• Always check limitation periods before filing appeals (Limitation Act, 1963)
• An Advocate-on-Record (AoR) is required to file cases in the Supreme Court
• High Court advocates can appear in any High Court with permission — Right of Audience under the Advocates Act
• Certified copies of the impugned order are mandatory for filing appeals
• Consider ADR (Mediation/Arbitration) before escalating — courts increasingly encourage it
• e-Filing is now available in the Supreme Court and most High Courts via ecourts.gov.in
