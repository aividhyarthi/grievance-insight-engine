Something has changed, but you can't quite point to one thing. Your period arrived four days early this month. Last Tuesday you woke at 3am with your heart racing for no reason. Your PMS in the second half of your cycle has gone from manageable to miserable. You feel anxious in a way that feels new and strange. You are 41, and you are wondering if this is the beginning of something.

The answer may well be yes. Perimenopause rarely announces itself dramatically. It begins as a collection of subtle shifts — in your cycle, your sleep, your mood — that are easy to attribute to other causes. In India, where the average age of menopause is 46 to 47, these early signs can begin in the early 40s or even late 30s. Knowing the pattern, and the order in which signs typically appear, is the first step to understanding what your body is telling you.

## Sign One: Your Cycle Quietly Gets Shorter

Before your periods become irregular, they often become slightly more frequent. A 29-day cycle becomes a 25-day cycle. A 27-day cycle becomes a 23-day cycle. This can feel like a blessing at first — your period arrives earlier than expected. But it is the first measurable signal of perimenopause: rising FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) is driving follicle development faster, compressing the follicular phase and shortening the overall cycle.

Most women don't notice this unless they're tracking carefully. If you've been using a cycle app, look back at the last six months. A consistent trend toward shorter cycles, without obvious cause, is worth noting.

## Sign Two: Periods Change Character — Often Heavier

Once cycle length shifts, the character of the period itself often follows. For many women, this means heavier periods, sometimes with clots that weren't there before. The cause is anovulation — cycles where oestrogen builds the uterine lining but no egg is released and no progesterone is produced to balance it. The resulting heavy bleed is called oestrogen dominance, and it is a defining early feature of perimenopause.

Some women experience the opposite — lighter, shorter periods, particularly if they were already prone to lighter cycles. There is no single "right" pattern. What matters is a meaningful change from your individual baseline.

## Sign Three: Sleep Begins to Fracture — Specifically at 2 to 4am

Early morning waking is one of the most consistent early signs of perimenopause, and one of the most misunderstood. It is not insomnia in the usual sense — you fall asleep normally, sleep for a few hours, then lie awake from 2 or 3am, often with a busy mind or a vague physical restlessness.

The driver is progesterone. Progesterone, through its metabolite allopregnanolone, acts on GABA receptors in the brain — the same pathway as sleep-promoting medications. As progesterone declines in the early perimenopausal phase, the brain's calming system loses some of its natural support. The early morning hours, when cortisol begins its natural pre-dawn rise, become a window of wakefulness.

This symptom often appears before any cycle irregularity, making it one of the earliest available signals.

## Sign Four: PMS Worsens in the Luteal Phase

If you've always had some premenstrual tension, perimenopause typically amplifies it. If you've had minimal PMS, it can appear as if from nowhere. The second half of the cycle — the luteal phase, roughly day 14 to the start of the next period — becomes increasingly uncomfortable: irritability that feels disproportionate, anxiety, tearfulness, bloating, breast tenderness, fatigue.

This happens because progesterone is the dominant hormone in the luteal phase, and as it declines, so does its moderating effect on the stress response. Allopregnanolone, progesterone's brain-active metabolite, normally reduces anxiety and irritability in the second half of the cycle. As it declines, the luteal phase becomes emotionally rawer.

Tracking whether your symptoms are cyclically worse in the ten to fourteen days before your period is one of the most useful things you can do to identify a hormonal pattern.

## Sign Five: New Anxiety or Panic-Like Episodes

Anxiety that feels qualitatively different from previous experience — more physical, harder to reason with, sometimes accompanied by a racing heart, tightness in the chest, or sudden heat — is a common early perimenopausal symptom. Some women describe it as their nervous system running "hot."

The mechanism involves both progesterone (as above) and oestrogen, which regulates serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine. As oestrogen begins to fluctuate erratically — rising too high then dropping sharply — the neurochemical baseline shifts with it. The result can present as generalised anxiety, mood instability, or panic-like episodes that are genuinely frightening if you don't know their source.

This is one of the symptoms most commonly misdiagnosed as a new anxiety disorder in perimenopausal women, particularly in the 40 to 44 age group.

## Sign Six: Breast Tenderness Before Your Period

Cyclical breast tenderness — tender, sometimes lumpy breasts in the week or two before your period — that is new or newly intense is another progesterone signature. Oestrogen promotes breast tissue proliferation; progesterone normally moderates it. As progesterone declines, the oestrogen-dominant environment leads to increased sensitivity and swelling in breast tissue during the luteal phase.

This is distinct from a persistent breast lump or change (which should always be assessed by a doctor). Cyclical breast tenderness that reliably comes and goes with the cycle is a hormonal pattern.

## Sign Seven: The First Faint Hot Flashes

For many women, vasomotor symptoms — hot flashes and night sweats — are associated with the later stages of perimenopause. But faint or mild heat episodes can begin early: a sudden flush of warmth, brief and mild, that doesn't quite register as a "hot flash" but is unusual enough to notice. You might attribute it to anxiety, the weather, or your morning chai.

These early vasomotor experiences are driven by oestrogen fluctuation — not just oestrogen decline. As oestrogen levels spike and then drop erratically, the hypothalamus (the brain's thermostat) becomes hypersensitive to small changes in body temperature, triggering heat responses disproportionate to the actual temperature change.

More pronounced hot flashes typically appear later in perimenopause, as oestrogen levels begin a more sustained decline, usually from the mid-40s onward in Indian women.

## Why Are These Signs Missed?

The pattern above — cycle changes, sleep disruption, worsening PMS, anxiety, breast tenderness, early heat episodes — spans months or even years before cycles become significantly irregular. Doctors (and women themselves) often attribute these symptoms to stress, thyroid problems, or "just aging."

This misattribution matters because:
1. It delays understanding and treatment
2. Women lose years of symptom management options
3. Underlying conditions like fibroids, iron deficiency, and thyroid dysfunction — which can both mimic and compound perimenopausal symptoms — go untested

## How to Track Your Symptoms in Relation to Your Cycle

Keeping a simple daily log for two to three months — cycle day, sleep quality, mood rating, energy, any specific symptoms — is one of the most valuable things you can do. A cyclical pattern (symptoms reliably worse in the luteal phase, better in the first two weeks of the cycle) strongly suggests hormonal involvement.

Bring this log to your gynaecologist appointment. Three months of tracked data is more informative than any single blood test during the perimenopause transition. If you want help organising your experience and preparing for that conversation, [our chat companion](/chat) is available.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Do you have to have hot flashes for it to be perimenopause?**
No. Hot flashes are the most well-known symptom, but they are often not the earliest. Many women spend years in perimenopause with cycle changes, sleep disruption, and mood symptoms without significant hot flashes. Hot flashes typically become more prominent in the later perimenopausal phase.

**2. Can perimenopause start while periods are still completely regular?**
Yes. Hormonal changes — particularly progesterone decline — can cause significant symptoms (sleep disruption, worsening PMS, anxiety) while cycles remain regular. Cycle irregularity often comes later in the transition.

**3. How do I know if it's perimenopause or thyroid problems?**
Several symptoms overlap. The key tests are thyroid function (TSH, free T4) and, separately, a hormonal panel (FSH, LH, oestradiol, progesterone). Both can be abnormal simultaneously — it's not uncommon for thyroid dysfunction and perimenopause to coexist. Both need to be checked.

**4. I'm 41 and having these signs. Should I be worried?**
Early perimenopause at 41 is not medically alarming — given that the average menopause age in India is 46 to 47, perimenopause beginning in the early 40s is within the normal Indian range. What matters is monitoring, managing symptoms, ruling out other conditions, and making sure you're not missing something treatable (like iron deficiency or thyroid issues).

**5. My symptoms are worse in the second half of my cycle. What does this mean?**
Symptoms that reliably worsen in the luteal phase (the two weeks before your period) and improve after the period starts are strongly indicative of low progesterone. This is a hallmark of early perimenopause. Discussing this pattern with a gynaecologist — along with a day 21 progesterone blood test — can help confirm it.
