You're 24, or 26, or 28. Your periods have become irregular — sometimes absent for two or three months at a stretch. You've been having hot flashes that feel out of place in someone your age. You're not sleeping well. When you mentioned it to your doctor, they ordered a pregnancy test and a thyroid panel. Both normal. And the conversation ended there.

If this is you, you deserve a more complete picture. True perimenopause in your 20s is not the same as perimenopause in your 40s — the mechanics are different, the name is different, and the path forward is different. What you may be experiencing is Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI), and getting the right diagnosis early is genuinely important for your long-term health.

## What Is Premature Ovarian Insufficiency — And Is It the Same as Early Menopause?

Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI) is defined as a loss of normal ovarian function before the age of 40. It affects approximately 1% of women under 40 and about 0.1% of women under 30. It is sometimes called premature menopause, but this term is misleading — and the distinction matters.

Unlike menopause, POI does not necessarily mean permanent ovarian failure. In many women with POI, the ovaries still function intermittently. Oestrogen can still be produced sporadically, and ovulation can still occur. Studies suggest that approximately 5 to 10% of women with POI conceive spontaneously after diagnosis. The ovaries are not "switched off" — they are functioning inconsistently and insufficiently.

This is why "perimenopause in your 20s" is not really the right framing. Typical perimenopause is the gradual decline of a previously fully functional ovarian system. POI involves a different, often more abrupt or earlier-onset insufficiency, often with a specific underlying cause.

## What Are the Symptoms of POI?

The symptoms overlap with perimenopause because the underlying hormonal changes are similar — low oestrogen, irregular or absent ovulation:

- **Irregular or absent periods**: Cycles that have become unpredictable, skipping months, or stopped entirely for four or more months.
- **Hot flashes and night sweats**: Sudden warmth, flushing, sweating — often dismissed as anxiety in young women.
- **Difficulty conceiving**: If you have been trying to conceive for six or more months without success, POI is one possibility worth investigating.
- **Vaginal dryness**: Less commonly noticed or reported in young women, but present.
- **Mood changes and anxiety**: Low oestrogen affects serotonin and GABA, leading to mood instability.
- **Sleep disruption**: Often driven by night sweats or the loss of progesterone's calming effect.
- **Brain fog and fatigue**: Common with low oestrogen and disrupted sleep.

## What Causes POI in Young Women?

POI is not caused by lifestyle factors, and it is not something you brought on yourself. The known causes include:

**Genetic factors**: Turner syndrome (a chromosomal condition affecting one X chromosome) is a well-known cause. Fragile X premutation — a genetic change on the X chromosome — is associated with POI in carriers. A family history of early menopause significantly increases the risk.

**Autoimmune conditions**: The immune system attacks ovarian tissue. This can occur in isolation or alongside other autoimmune conditions like thyroid disease (Hashimoto's), Type 1 diabetes, or Addison's disease. For this reason, a thyroid and adrenal evaluation is important at diagnosis.

**Cancer treatment**: Chemotherapy and radiation to the pelvic area can damage the ovaries. The extent depends on the type and dose of treatment, and the age at which treatment occurred.

**Idiopathic**: In many cases, no identifiable cause is found.

## Why Does Early Diagnosis Matter?

If you have POI and it's not identified and treated, the long-term consequences extend well beyond fertility:

**Bone health**: Oestrogen is essential for maintaining bone density. Low oestrogen in your 20s, if untreated, accelerates bone loss. Women with untreated POI have significantly higher risk of osteoporosis and fractures later in life. This risk is real and preventable.

**Cardiovascular health**: Oestrogen has a protective effect on the cardiovascular system. Early menopause — natural or from POI — is associated with higher cardiovascular risk in later life, particularly if untreated.

**Brain health**: There is emerging evidence that oestrogen plays a role in cognitive protection. Women with untreated POI may have higher risk of cognitive decline.

**Mental health**: The psychological impact of a POI diagnosis — particularly the fertility implications — is significant and often underestimated. Support matters.

## How Is POI Diagnosed?

Diagnosis requires two elevated FSH readings (above 25 IU/L in most labs, above 40 IU/L is typically diagnostic) taken at least four weeks apart, alongside amenorrhoea (absent periods) or oligomenorrhoea (very infrequent periods) for at least four months, in a woman under 40.

Additional tests at diagnosis typically include: LH, oestradiol, karyotype (chromosomal analysis), fragile X premutation testing, adrenal antibodies, thyroid antibodies, and a bone density scan (DEXA scan).

## What Happens After a POI Diagnosis?

**Hormonal support is important**, not optional. Unlike menopause in the natural timing (age 46 to 52), POI before 40 represents a hormonal deficiency that carries real health risks. Hormonal support — whether through the pill or other forms — is generally recommended until the average age of natural menopause (around 50 to 51), to protect bones, heart, and brain.

**Fertility options**: If you wish to conceive, specialist fertility care is important. Options depend on whether you still have some ovarian function and include assisted reproduction with your own or donor eggs.

**Mental health support**: A diagnosis of POI in your 20s is a major life event. ## What If You're Under 40 but Still Cycling?

Not all irregular cycles in the late 20s or 30s indicate POI. Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), thyroid dysfunction, stress, significant weight change, and hypothalamic amenorrhoea can all cause irregular periods. A proper hormonal evaluation — including FSH on day 2 to 3 of a cycle, LH, oestradiol, AMH, thyroid, and prolactin — is the way to distinguish between them. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I'm 27 and my periods have become irregular. Is this definitely POI?**
Irregular periods in your late 20s have several possible causes — PCOS, thyroid problems, stress, weight changes, and sometimes POI. A proper hormonal evaluation with a gynaecologist will help identify the cause. Don't self-diagnose, but do advocate for thorough testing.

**2. Does POI mean I definitely can't have children?**
Not necessarily. Ovarian function in POI is intermittent, not always completely absent. Around 5 to 10% of women with POI conceive spontaneously. Fertility specialists can also discuss assisted options. This is a conversation worth having with a specialist, not a conclusion to draw before you've had a full evaluation.

**3. Can I just use contraceptive pills to treat POI?**
The combined pill provides oestrogen and progestogen and does protect bones and manage symptoms. However, if you're under the care of a specialist for POI, they may recommend specific hormonal therapies rather than just the pill, particularly as the fertility question is different. Discuss your specific situation with a gynaecologist or reproductive endocrinologist.

**4. Is POI hereditary?**
Yes, in many cases. If your mother or a maternal female relative had early menopause, your risk is higher. Genetic factors — including fragile X premutation — can be tested for. This is especially relevant if you have a family history or are planning a family.
