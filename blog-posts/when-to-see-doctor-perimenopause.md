Many women go through months of symptoms — disrupted sleep, irregular cycles, new anxiety, worsening PMS — before they consider raising it with a doctor. And when they do, they often encounter dismissal: "You're too young for that," or "This is just stress," or "Your tests are normal." The first question — when to go — is surprisingly hard to answer if nobody has told you what the thresholds are.

The answer is different depending on your age. The urgency is genuinely different at 29, at 30, and at 35. Here's a clear breakdown of what symptoms at each age should prompt, what to ask for, and how to navigate a healthcare system where perimenopausal literacy is still uneven.

## If You Are 29 — Seek Prompt Attention

At 29, the standard perimenopausal timeline does not apply. If you are experiencing irregular periods, absent periods, hot flashes, night sweats, difficulty conceiving after six months of trying, or significant unexplained fatigue alongside cycle changes, do not wait and see.

These symptoms in a 29-year-old can indicate Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI), a condition where ovarian function is insufficient for age. POI affects roughly 1% of women under 40. It is not the same as typical perimenopause — but it has similar hormonal consequences, and diagnosis matters enormously for your long-term bone, cardiovascular, and brain health.

**What to ask for at 29:**
- FSH on day 2 to 3 of your cycle (repeated four weeks later if elevated)
- LH and oestradiol
- Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)
- Thyroid function (TSH, free T4)
- Prolactin (elevated prolactin can cause irregular periods and is treatable)
- If FSH is elevated on two occasions four weeks apart alongside cycle disruption: karyotype, fragile X premutation testing, adrenal antibody screen

See a gynaecologist, not just your general physician, for this evaluation.

## If You Are 30 — Same Urgency

The clinical thresholds for POI apply until age 40. A 30-year-old with irregular or absent periods, hot flashes, or significant hormonal symptoms should be evaluated with the same panel as above. The fact that you are "only 30" is not reassurance — it is precisely the reason to take it seriously.

At 30, the other conditions that can cause irregular periods — PCOS, thyroid dysfunction, hyperprolactinaemia, hypothalamic amenorrhoea from stress or low body weight — also need to be evaluated. These are all conditions where early treatment makes a significant difference.

**Do not let a doctor tell you this is just stress without running tests.** Stress can disrupt cycles. But so can multiple medical conditions. A symptom that looks like stress without investigation is just an uninvestigated symptom.

## If You Are 35 — Watch and Act

At 35, the picture changes. You are old enough that early perimenopause is a genuine clinical possibility — particularly in the Indian context, where the average menopause age is 46 to 47 and perimenopause can begin in the mid-to-late 30s. At the same time, you are technically below the POI threshold (under 40), so the most alarming possibilities are less likely than at 29.

**See a gynaecologist if you have had for three or more months:**
- Worsening PMS that is affecting your quality of life
- Heavier periods than your baseline
- New sleep disruption — particularly early morning waking
- New anxiety that is cyclical or unexplained
- Shorter cycles (under 24 days) when your usual is 27 to 29

These symptoms together, persisting for several months, are worth a hormonal conversation. A full panel including FSH, LH, oestradiol, Day 21 progesterone, TSH, ferritin, B12, and vitamin D gives a useful picture and rules out common overlapping conditions.

At 35, you do not need to frame this as an emergency — but you do need to be taken seriously.

## Red Flags at Any Age

Regardless of whether you are 29, 35, or 44, certain symptoms warrant prompt medical evaluation — not a wait-and-see approach:

- **Complete absence of periods for three or more months** in a woman not on hormonal contraception
- **Soaking a pad fully every hour for two or more consecutive hours** (this is heavy bleeding requiring investigation)
- **New severe depression or suicidal thoughts**: Call iCall on 9152987821 or go to an emergency department. This is a medical emergency. Perimenopause can precipitate significant mental health crises and needs direct support.
- **Severe hot flashes that are significantly disrupting your ability to work or sleep** for sustained periods
- **Bleeding after sex (post-coital bleeding)**: This is not a normal perimenopausal symptom and needs evaluation

## What to Say to Your Doctor

Many women walk into appointments and feel their symptoms minimised or redirected. Coming prepared helps. Here is a structure that works:

1. **Lead with duration and pattern**: "For the last four months, I have been waking at 3am most nights and my periods have been heavier. This is different from my previous baseline."
2. **Mention the cyclical nature if relevant**: "My anxiety and mood changes are worse in the two weeks before my period and improve once it starts."
3. **Ask specific questions**: "I would like to check my FSH, oestradiol, Day 21 progesterone, TSH, and ferritin. Can we do that?"
4. **Bring written notes or a symptom diary**: Three months of tracked symptoms is more compelling than describing them from memory.

## The Dismissal Problem in Indian Healthcare

Women in India face specific barriers when seeking care for perimenopausal symptoms:

**Age-based dismissal**: "You're too young for menopause" — often said to women in their late 30s and 40s without regard for the fact that Indian women reach menopause earlier on average.

**Emotional reframing**: Symptoms attributed to family stress, workload, or anxiety without testing the hormonal hypothesis.

**Limited specialist awareness**: Not all gynaecologists are equally informed about perimenopause management. Some have stronger surgical practices than endocrine ones.

**Cultural silence**: Many Indian women do not have language for menopause beyond the vague and occasionally shameful "change of life." The absence of cultural normalisation makes it harder to walk into a clinic and name what is happening.

## How to Find a Menopause-Informed Gynaecologist in India

Ask for a gynaecologist with an interest in endocrinology or reproductive health, not just obstetrics. Academic medical centres and larger private hospitals in metropolitan areas tend to have specialists in this area. Online communities — including ours — are a good source of peer recommendations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I'm 32 and my doctor says my FSH is normal. Can I still be in early perimenopause?**
Normal FSH at 32 in the presence of still-regular cycles does not confirm perimenopause — but it also doesn't fully exclude early hormonal shifts. If your symptoms are significant (worsening PMS, sleep changes, new anxiety), request a Day 21 progesterone alongside the FSH. Progesterone changes often precede FSH elevation in early perimenopause.

**2. My periods are irregular but my doctor says it's PCOS. How do I know which it is?**
PCOS and early perimenopause/POI can look similar symptomatically, but they have different hormonal profiles. In PCOS, AMH is typically elevated, LH is often relatively high compared to FSH, and androgens may be elevated. In POI/early perimenopause, FSH rises and AMH falls. A full hormonal panel will help distinguish them.

**3. Can I just go straight to a gynaecologist, or do I need a referral first?**
In most private healthcare settings in India, you can see a gynaecologist directly without a referral. In government healthcare settings, a referral may be needed. Going directly to a gynaecologist with specialist interest in hormonal and reproductive health is often more efficient than starting with a general physician for this specific concern.

**4. I'm experiencing severe depression alongside my irregular periods. What should I do first?**
Seek mental health support urgently alongside the hormonal workup. Call iCall on 9152987821 or speak to a psychiatrist or counsellor. The two investigations can happen simultaneously. Perimenopausal hormonal changes can precipitate or worsen depression, and both the mental health and the hormonal dimensions deserve attention.

**5. My mother had early menopause. Does that mean I will too?**
A family history of early menopause is one of the strongest predictors of your own timing. If your mother reached menopause before 45, your risk of similar timing is meaningfully elevated. This is a reason to monitor symptoms more proactively and have a lower threshold for a hormonal evaluation in your late 30s and early 40s.
