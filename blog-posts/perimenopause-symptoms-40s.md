Your periods have been arriving a few days earlier than usual. You're waking at 3am and lying there, mind running, unable to get back to sleep. Your PMS feels worse than it did at 32. And last month, your period was so heavy you had to cancel a day of meetings. You're 43, and something has shifted — but nobody told you it would start this soon.

In India, the average age of menopause is 46 to 47 years — four to five years earlier than the Western average of 51 to 52. This means perimenopause, the transitional phase that precedes the final period, often begins in the late 30s to early 40s for Indian women. If you're 40 and noticing changes, you're not imagining things, and you're not "too young." You may be right on schedule.

## What Does Your Cycle Actually Start Doing First?

The earliest signal is usually a shortening of the cycle. Instead of a 28 to 30-day cycle, you might notice 24 to 26-day cycles for several months. This happens because declining progesterone levels shorten the luteal phase — the second half of your cycle after ovulation. Later, cycles become erratic: sometimes short, sometimes long, sometimes skipped altogether.

Heavy periods often follow. When cycles become anovulatory (no egg is released despite bleeding), there's no corpus luteum, and therefore no progesterone. Oestrogen continues to build up the uterine lining unchecked, and when it finally sheds, the result is a heavy, sometimes clotty bleed. This is called oestrogen dominance, and it's one of the defining features of early perimenopause.

## Why Are You Waking Up Between 2 and 4am?

This one surprises almost everyone. Sleep disruption — specifically early morning waking — is one of the most consistent early symptoms of perimenopause, and it's directly linked to progesterone decline.

Progesterone acts on GABA receptors in the brain, the same receptors targeted by anti-anxiety medications and sleep aids. When progesterone levels drop, the calming effect on the nervous system diminishes. The result: you may fall asleep fine, but wake at 2 or 3am, mind alert, unable to settle. This is not insomnia in the conventional sense. It's a hormonal pattern, and it often precedes the hot flashes that many women associate with menopause.

Oestrogen also plays a role in regulating body temperature. As oestrogen levels fluctuate (they don't decline steadily — they spike and crash erratically during perimenopause), the hypothalamus becomes hypersensitive to small changes in body temperature, triggering hot flashes and night sweats that can further disrupt sleep.

## Is Your Anxiety New — or Just Much Worse?

Many women in their early 40s describe a new quality of anxiety — a jitteriness, a tendency to catastrophise, a sense of dread before situations that never bothered them before. This is not a personality change. It is, again, largely progesterone.

Progesterone's metabolite allopregnanolone directly modulates the stress response. When progesterone declines, the buffer against cortisol diminishes. Everyday stressors hit harder. Some women describe it as feeling like their nervous system is running hot all the time.

Oestrogen also affects serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine. As oestrogen levels swing unpredictably, so does mood. The result can look like depression, generalised anxiety, or simply feeling like yourself on a bad day — except it's most days now.

## When Do the Hot Flashes Begin?

For most women, vasomotor symptoms — hot flashes and night sweats — are more pronounced from the mid-40s onwards, typically when oestrogen levels begin to decline more significantly rather than just fluctuate. In your early 40s, you may notice warmth or flushing rather than the dramatic hot flashes of late perimenopause.

That said, every woman is different. Some experience intense hot flashes from age 42 or 43. Others have minimal vasomotor symptoms throughout. Indian women, on the whole, report hot flashes at slightly lower rates than Western women in some studies, though symptoms of mood disruption and sleep problems are reported at similar or higher rates.

## What's Happening to Your Weight and Skin?

The belly fat that seems to have arrived uninvited is not about eating more. It is about where fat is deposited as oestrogen declines. Oestrogen influences fat distribution, and as levels change, fat tends to shift from the hips and thighs to the abdomen — a metabolic pattern associated with increased cardiovascular and insulin resistance risk.

Skin changes are also common: dryness, reduced elasticity, occasional breakouts (a progesterone and oestrogen imbalance can trigger adult acne). The collagen in skin contains oestrogen receptors, and declining oestrogen affects skin thickness and moisture.

## What Tests Are Actually Worth Getting?

If you're in your 40s with these symptoms, the following blood tests give the most useful picture:

- **FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone)**: Rising FSH indicates the ovaries are being stimulated harder to produce eggs. A single test is not definitive — FSH fluctuates wildly in perimenopause — but a pattern over time is meaningful.
- **LH (luteinising hormone)**: Often rises alongside FSH.
- **Oestradiol**: Fluctuates, so a single reading is limited, but very low levels suggest later perimenopause.
- **Thyroid (TSH, free T3, free T4)**: Thyroid dysfunction is common in Indian women and shares many symptoms with perimenopause — fatigue, weight changes, mood disruption, irregular periods. It must be ruled out.
- **Ferritin**: Heavy periods deplete iron stores. Low ferritin causes fatigue, brain fog, breathlessness, and worsening sleep — symptoms that mimic and compound perimenopause.
- **Vitamin B12 and Vitamin D**: Both are frequently low in Indian women, and deficiencies worsen fatigue, brain fog, and mood.

Note that a normal FSH does not rule out perimenopause. Hormones are most usefully interpreted alongside symptoms and cycle patterns, not in isolation.

## What Actually Helps?

**Food**: Indian diets are, usefully, already rich in phytoestrogens — plant compounds that have a mild oestrogen-like effect. Soy (tofu, soy milk), lentils, ragi (finger millet, an excellent source of calcium and phytoestrogens), flaxseed (add a tablespoon ground to dahi or roti dough), and sesame seeds all support hormonal balance. Dark leafy greens, nuts, and seeds provide magnesium, which supports sleep and reduces anxiety.

**Exercise**: Strength training has the strongest evidence for perimenopausal women. It supports bone density (which begins to decline as oestrogen falls), improves insulin sensitivity, boosts mood through endorphins, and helps manage the abdominal weight gain. Even two sessions per week make a measurable difference. Walking and yoga support stress management and joint health.

**Sleep hygiene**: Keeping the bedroom cool, avoiding screens and alcohol within two hours of sleep, and consistent sleep-wake times all help. The 2 to 4am window is harder to address without hormone support, but a cool bedroom, magnesium-rich foods (spinach, pumpkin seeds, almonds), and avoiding stimulants after noon can help.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I'm 41 and my periods are still regular. Can I still be in perimenopause?**
Yes. Perimenopause can begin with subtle hormonal changes — worsening PMS, new sleep disruption, increased anxiety — while cycles remain relatively regular. Cycle regularity is one of the last things to change for many women.

**2. My doctor says my FSH is normal, so it's not perimenopause. Are they right?**
FSH fluctuates significantly during perimenopause, and a single "normal" reading doesn't rule it out. Perimenopause is a clinical diagnosis based on symptoms and cycle patterns, not a single blood test. If your symptoms fit, it's worth a second conversation with a gynaecologist familiar with perimenopause.

**3. Is perimenopause in the early 40s normal in India?**
Yes. With the average age of menopause in India being 46 to 47, perimenopause beginning in the early 40s is entirely consistent with Indian norms. It is not a sign that something has gone wrong.

**4. I'm having heavier periods than ever before. Is that normal in perimenopause?**
Heavier periods are extremely common in perimenopause, caused by anovulatory cycles and oestrogen dominance. However, very heavy bleeding that soaks a pad every hour for consecutive hours, or passes large clots, warrants a gynaecology review to rule out fibroids, polyps, or other causes.

**5. Will the anxiety and brain fog get better?**
For most women, these symptoms fluctuate with hormonal shifts and tend to stabilise after menopause. In the meantime, addressing sleep, exercise, stress management, and nutritional deficiencies can make a significant difference. Some women find hormonal support helpful — this is worth discussing with a menopause-informed gynaecologist.
