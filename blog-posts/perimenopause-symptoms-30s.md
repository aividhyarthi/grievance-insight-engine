You're 37, your periods have been heavier for the past few months, and you're waking up at 3am more nights than not. Your PMS has become almost unbearable — the anxiety, the irritability, the breast tenderness that starts a full week before your period. You mention it to your doctor, who runs a thyroid test. Normal. She puts it down to stress. But it doesn't feel like just stress.

Here's what's rarely said plainly: yes, perimenopause symptoms can begin in the late 30s. The hormonal changes that precede menopause are not a sudden event — they are a gradual process that can start quietly, a decade or more before the final period. For Indian women, whose average menopause age is 46 to 47, symptoms beginning at 36 or 38 are entirely plausible.

## What Makes the Late 30s Different From Typical Perimenopause?

When most people picture perimenopause, they imagine women in their late 40s with irregular periods and hot flashes. But the earliest phase of the transition — sometimes called the early menopausal transition or late reproductive stage — involves changes in cycle length and luteal phase quality long before cycles become visibly irregular.

The luteal phase is the second half of your menstrual cycle, after ovulation. In your mid-to-late 30s, the number of viable follicles in your ovaries begins to decline more noticeably, and with it, the quality of ovulation changes. The corpus luteum (the temporary structure that forms after ovulation and produces progesterone) becomes less robust. Progesterone levels in the second half of the cycle begin to fall.

This is why women in their late 30s often notice worsening PMS, heavier periods, and sleep disruption first — these are the hallmarks of declining progesterone, which is the first hormone to fall in the perimenopausal process.

## What Are the Symptoms to Look Out For?

The symptom profile at 35 to 39 tends to be progesterone-dominant in its presentation:

**Worsening PMS**: Irritability, anxiety, bloating, breast tenderness, and mood disruption in the week before your period that feels qualitatively worse than it used to. Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) can emerge or worsen during this time.

**Heavier periods**: Often the first thing women notice. Periods that used to be manageable become heavy, sometimes with clots. This is the oestrogen dominance effect — less progesterone means the uterine lining builds up more than it should.

**Shorter cycles**: Cycles may shift from 28 to 30 days to 24 to 26 days. This is one of the earliest measurable signals of perimenopause and is tied to a shortened follicular phase driven by rising FSH.

**Sleep disruption**: Specifically early morning waking — lying awake from 2 to 4am. This is directly tied to progesterone's role in the GABA system, which regulates relaxation and sleep.

**New or worsening anxiety**: A nervous quality, a lower stress threshold, more intense reactions to ordinary pressures. Allopregnanolone — a metabolite of progesterone — normally buffers the stress response. As progesterone declines, this buffer shrinks.

**Breast tenderness**: Often more pronounced and lasting longer into the cycle.

**Brain fog**: Difficulty concentrating, word-finding problems, a general sense of mental haziness in the days around ovulation or in the luteal phase.

## Why Do Doctors Miss This?

There are several reasons why perimenopause in the late 30s is consistently under-recognised.

First, routine blood tests — TSH for thyroid, complete blood count, ferritin — are ordered, and when they come back normal, symptoms are attributed to stress or lifestyle. FSH is not typically tested in a woman in her late 30s unless she is trying to conceive and having trouble.

Second, there is a cultural and clinical assumption that perimenopause is a problem of the late 40s. The word itself signals "about 50" in most people's minds. When a 37-year-old describes these symptoms, perimenopause is often not on the differential.

Third, many symptoms — anxiety, sleep disruption, fatigue, mood changes — are explained by other life pressures. A woman in her late 30s in India is often managing a career, children, parents, and household. Her symptoms are assumed to be the product of her circumstances, not her biology.

## Is This Premature Ovarian Insufficiency?

It is important to understand the difference between early perimenopause and premature ovarian insufficiency (POI).

**Early perimenopause (35 to 39)** means that the hormonal changes of the menopausal transition have begun, but ovarian function continues. Cycles may be irregular or symptomatic, but they are still occurring. This is a normal biological variation, especially relevant in the Indian context where menopause comes earlier on average.

**Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)** is defined as ovarian insufficiency in a woman under 40 — irregular or absent periods for four or more months combined with elevated FSH on two occasions, four weeks apart. POI affects about 1% of women under 40. Importantly, POI does not always mean permanent cessation of ovarian function — intermittent function can continue, and spontaneous pregnancy is possible in around 5 to 10% of cases.

If you're in your late 30s with worsening PMS and heavier periods but still cycling regularly, POI is unlikely. If your periods have stopped or become very infrequent, evaluation for POI is warranted.

## What to Track

If you suspect early perimenopause, begin tracking systematically. A cycle tracking app or a simple paper diary noting the following is useful:

- Cycle length (first day of one period to the first day of the next)
- Flow heaviness (light / moderate / heavy / very heavy)
- Luteal phase symptoms (mood, sleep, anxiety, breast tenderness)
- Sleep quality, especially night waking
- Any mid-cycle symptoms

Bring three to six months of this data to a gynaecologist appointment. It is far more useful than a single blood test.

## When to See a Gynaecologist

See a gynaecologist if:
- Your periods have been consistently heavier for three or more months
- You are waking frequently at night and are not sure why
- You have new or worsening anxiety that is affecting your daily life
- Your cycle has shortened significantly
- You have any period cessation for more than two months

Tell the gynaecologist you want an evaluation that includes FSH, LH, oestradiol, progesterone on Day 21 of your cycle (or seven days before your expected period), TSH, ferritin, Vitamin D, and B12. You are entitled to ask for a full hormonal picture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I'm 36 and my periods have become heavier. Is this actually perimenopause or could it be something else?**
Heavier periods in the late 30s can have multiple causes — fibroids (very common in Indian women in this age group), polyps, thyroid issues, or perimenopause-related oestrogen dominance. It is worth seeing a gynaecologist for an ultrasound and blood work to identify the cause.

**2. My FSH came back normal. Does that rule out perimenopause?**
Not definitively. FSH fluctuates significantly in early perimenopause and a single normal reading doesn't exclude it. Symptoms and cycle patterns matter more at this stage than a single hormone test.

**3. Can early perimenopause affect my fertility?**
Declining ovarian reserve can affect fertility. If you are in your late 30s, experiencing these symptoms, and planning to conceive, it is worth an AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone) test and antral follicle count alongside a fertility assessment. Earlier perimenopause does not make conception impossible, but timing becomes more relevant.

**4. My doctor says I'm too young for perimenopause. What should I do?**
Ask your doctor to explain what else might explain your specific combination of symptoms. If your symptoms strongly suggest hormonal changes — worsening PMS, heavier periods, sleep disruption, new anxiety — and thyroid and iron have been ruled out, ask for a referral to a gynaecologist or gynaecological endocrinologist with experience in reproductive hormones.

**5. Will these symptoms get worse before they get better?**
They can. Perimenopause is not a linear process — symptoms can fluctuate for several years before stabilising after menopause. However, knowing what's driving your symptoms allows you to address them: through lifestyle changes, nutritional support, and, when appropriate, medical support.
