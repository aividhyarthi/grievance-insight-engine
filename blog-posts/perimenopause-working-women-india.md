It is 11am on a Wednesday in a Bengaluru tech office. Pramila, 44, is mid-sentence in a presentation to her team when she loses the thread. Not metaphorically — the word she was reaching for simply isn't there. She covers, carries on, finishes the presentation. In the bathroom afterwards, she notices the flush still on her face. She's been awake since 3am. Her period started today, heavy, and she's changed her pad twice since the commute. She has three more meetings before lunch.

This is not a crisis. This is Tuesday — or Wednesday, or any day — for a vast and invisible cohort of India's working women who are navigating perimenopause in full professional stride without anyone knowing, without any workplace accommodation, and often without even a name for what is happening to them.

## How Perimenopause Actually Shows Up at Work

The workplace manifestations of perimenopause are specific and worth naming:

**Brain fog during high-stakes moments**: The word-finding difficulty, the mental gap during a meeting, the inability to hold three things in working memory simultaneously, the difficulty recalling details that used to come easily. This is neurological — oestrogen supports cognitive function, and its fluctuation affects the prefrontal cortex's working memory and retrieval systems. It is not a sign of incompetence. It is a hormonal symptom.

**Anxiety before or during meetings**: A racing heart, shallow breathing, the sense that something is wrong before a client call or a presentation — disproportionate to the actual stakes. Women who have been confident public performers for years find themselves dreading interactions that used to be easy. This is the GABA system, disrupted by falling progesterone.

**Heavy periods requiring logistical management**: Planning the week around the first two days of a period. Keeping spare clothes at work. Knowing which client meetings cannot be scheduled for Tuesday and Wednesday. Ducking out between sessions to change. The cognitive load of managing all of this, invisibly, while maintaining professional output.

**Sleep deprivation**: Much of the cognitive impairment working women describe in perimenopause is downstream of sleep. Waking at 2 or 3am repeatedly means arriving to work already in deficit — slower processing, lower frustration tolerance, poorer memory consolidation. Sleep deprivation over months accumulates in a way that feels like a personal failing but is physiological.

**Temperature regulation at work**: Air-conditioned offices can be profoundly uncomfortable for women in late perimenopause. The sudden heat of a hot flash in an air-conditioned meeting room — combined with the social awkwardness of not being able to explain it — is an additional layer of discomfort.

**Mood variability that feels unprofessional**: The shorter fuse. The tearfulness at feedback that wouldn't have landed this way three years ago. The difficulty maintaining the professional mask through the second half of the cycle when progesterone has bottomed out and oestrogen is erratic. Most women manage this flawlessly from the outside and carry the cost internally.

## The Invisible Load

What makes this particularly difficult is that perimenopausal symptoms at work carry an additional burden: they are invisible, unspoken, and often internalised as personal failure.

Fatigue from heavy bleeding is attributed to not exercising enough. Brain fog is taken as evidence of declining ability. Anxiety before meetings is labelled a confidence problem. The mood variability in the luteal phase is explained as "stress management issues." None of these framings are accurate, and all of them cause damage — to self-perception, to career confidence, and to the working relationships where women cannot admit what is happening.

India's workplace culture adds further complexity. Menopause is not spoken about in most Indian professional environments. There are no frameworks, no HR policies, no menopause champions. The woman experiencing a hot flash during a board presentation has no recourse to acknowledgment, adjustment, or support.

## Practical Strategies That Actually Help

**For brain fog:**
- Write things down immediately — don't rely on working memory for things that matter
- Use structured agendas and notes in meetings rather than speaking extemporaneously when possible
- Schedule cognitively demanding work for your best time of day (typically morning, and typically in the first half of your cycle)
- Break large tasks into smaller documented steps
- The fog is usually worst in the luteal phase and during sleep-deprived periods — if you can plan around this, do

**For heavy periods at work:**
- Keep a small emergency kit at your desk (spare clothes, additional products, pain relief)
- When possible, schedule client-facing or travel-heavy days around your cycle — not always possible, but worth doing when there is flexibility
- Period underwear as a backup layer significantly reduces the anxiety of flooding
- Advocate for private, accessible bathroom facilities if your workplace lacks them

**For sleep deprivation:**
- Protect the 10pm to midnight window aggressively — falling asleep earlier gives you a longer total window before the 3am disruption
- Avoid alcohol, even one glass — it significantly disrupts sleep architecture in the second half of the night
- A cool bedroom (or a fan) mitigates both hot flash-driven and progesterone-driven night waking
- Magnesium-rich foods in the evening (pumpkin seeds, spinach, dark chocolate in moderation, almonds) support sleep

**For anxiety:**
- Structured breathwork before difficult meetings: four counts in, hold four, out for six — this activates the parasympathetic system within minutes
- Identifying the cyclical pattern of anxiety (is it consistently worse in days 18 to 28 of your cycle?) helps enormously in not taking it at face value
- Brief, scheduled breaks during demanding days — even five minutes away from screens — reduce cortisol accumulation

**For hot flashes at work:**
- Layering clothing for rapid adjustment
- Keeping a small fan at your desk if possible
- Identifying and avoiding triggers where you can (hot drinks, spicy foods, alcohol, stress — the latter being inevitable, but manageable at the margins)
- Notifying a trusted colleague if you feel comfortable — not to explain everything, but to have an ally who understands

## The Career Cost

Research from the UK Menopause at Work survey estimated that one in ten women left their job because of menopausal symptoms, and three in ten reduced their working hours or declined a promotion. Indian-specific data is limited, but there is no reason to assume the pattern is radically different. The experience of working through perimenopause without support or acknowledgment has measurable career consequences.

This is not inevitable. Symptom management — through lifestyle measures, medical support where appropriate, and workplace adjustments — can significantly reduce the functional impact. But it requires acknowledging the problem first.

## The Conversation With Your Employer

In most Indian workplaces, this is genuinely difficult. Menopause is not recognised in most HR policies, and the cultural silence makes it hard to raise. A few approaches that working women have found useful:

- **Framing it as a health condition without detailed disclosure**: "I'm managing a health condition that is affecting my sleep. I'd benefit from flexibility around early start times when needed" is entirely reasonable.
- **Talking to a trusted manager or HR contact**: Not all workplaces are hostile to this conversation. Some women find that a private conversation about needing modest accommodations — access to a cool space, schedule flexibility during heavy bleeding — is received better than expected.
- **Seeking out other women**: Identifying colleagues in similar life stages who are navigating the same experience. The isolation of thinking you're alone in this is itself a burden.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can I ask my employer for adjustments related to perimenopause in India?**
Currently, India does not have specific workplace legislation around menopause. However, general provisions around health and workplace welfare apply. Practically, flexible working, access to cooling spaces, and schedule adjustments during heavy bleeding are reasonable requests. The conversation is easier in organisations with progressive HR cultures; in others, framing it as "a medical condition" without specifying details may be more practical.

**2. My brain fog is affecting my confidence at work. What can I do?**
First: it is neurological, not a sign that your abilities have changed. Most women report that cognitive symptoms improve significantly once hormonal fluctuations stabilise — either naturally after menopause or with appropriate support. In the meantime, compensatory strategies (notes, prepared materials, structured agendas) reduce the gap between what your brain can do and what it feels like it's doing.

**3. I'm worried about disclosing perimenopause to my employer. What are the risks?**
This is a legitimate concern in some workplace cultures. You are not obliged to disclose a medical diagnosis. If you need accommodations, you can describe functional limitations ("I need flexibility for a medical appointment," "I may need access to a cool space") without naming the condition.

**4. My performance reviews have been suffering. What should I do?**
If perimenopausal symptoms are affecting your work performance, treating those symptoms — through lifestyle measures, and if needed, medical support — is the most direct intervention. Speak to a gynaecologist about symptom management options. In parallel, consider whether there is a trusted person in your workplace with whom a broader conversation is possible.

**5. Are there any Indian companies with menopause policies?**
A small but growing number of multinational companies operating in India, and some progressive Indian employers, have begun introducing menopause support policies, flexible working provisions, and awareness training. This is a developing space. If your company does not have one, you could be the person who advocates for it — that conversation starts with individual women raising it with HR.
