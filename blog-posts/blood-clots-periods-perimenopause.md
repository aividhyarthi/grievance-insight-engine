You open the bathroom door and find what looks like a crime scene. The clots are alarming — dark, thick, sometimes the size of a coin. If you're in your early to mid-40s and this is new, you're probably frightened, and you're almost certainly wondering what is wrong. The answer, in most cases, is: nothing catastrophic — but something worth understanding, tracking, and sometimes treating.

Blood clots during periods in perimenopause are among the most common and underreported symptoms of this life stage. They have a specific hormonal cause, and that cause is almost entirely reversible as the body eventually moves through perimenopause. But there are warning signs that require prompt medical attention, and there are underlying conditions — particularly fibroids, which are extremely common among Indian women — that deserve investigation.

## Why Do Blood Clots Happen in Perimenopause?

The direct cause is anovulation. During perimenopause, cycles increasingly become anovulatory — the ovaries produce oestrogen and the uterine lining builds up, but no egg is released. When there is no ovulation, there is no corpus luteum, and therefore no progesterone is produced.

Progesterone is the hormone that signals the uterine lining to stop thickening and prepare for shedding. Without it, oestrogen continues building the lining unchecked — a state called oestrogen dominance. The lining becomes thicker than usual, and when it finally does shed, it sheds heavily, sometimes in large pieces.

The body produces a natural anticoagulant during periods to prevent clotting during normal blood flow. But when flow is very heavy — as it is with a thick, anovulatory lining — the anticoagulant cannot keep up, and the blood partially clots before it can be expelled. That is what the clots you're seeing represent.

## What Is "Normal" Versus Concerning?

Not all clots are the same. Here is a useful guide:

**Generally within the range of perimenopausal variation:**
- Clots smaller than a two-rupee coin
- Passing clots on your heaviest one or two days only
- Flow that is heavy but manageable with a pad or tampon that lasts two or more hours

**Worth seeing a gynaecologist about:**
- Clots consistently larger than a two-rupee coin
- Soaking a pad or tampon fully every one to two hours for two or more consecutive hours
- Periods lasting longer than seven days
- Bleeding between periods
- Pelvic pain, pressure, or bloating alongside heavy bleeding
- Anaemia symptoms: fatigue, breathlessness, dizziness, pallor

**Seek urgent medical care (call 112 or go to an emergency department) if:**
- You are soaking through a pad every thirty minutes for several consecutive hours
- You feel faint, dizzy, or have chest pain alongside heavy bleeding

## What Else Could Be Causing This?

Heavy, clotty periods in perimenopausal women should always prompt consideration of other causes alongside the hormonal explanation:

**Fibroids (uterine leiomyomata)**: These benign muscle tumours of the uterus are extremely common in Indian women — studies suggest they affect up to 25 to 40% of women of reproductive age, with peak incidence in the late 30s and 40s. Fibroids grow under the influence of oestrogen, which means the oestrogen-dominant perimenopausal environment can cause them to enlarge. Submucosal fibroids (inside the uterine cavity) are particularly associated with heavy, clotty bleeding. An ultrasound will identify them.

**Adenomyosis**: The uterine lining grows into the muscle wall of the uterus. This causes heavy, painful, clotty periods and a slightly enlarged, "boggy" uterus. It is common in women in their 40s and often coexists with fibroids.

**Endometrial polyps**: Small outgrowths of the uterine lining that can cause heavy bleeding and spotting between periods. Identifiable on ultrasound, often with saline infusion sonography.

**Thyroid dysfunction**: Both hypothyroidism and occasionally hyperthyroidism can cause heavy periods. This should be checked with a blood test.

## The Iron Connection — This One Is Important

Heavy periods are the leading cause of iron deficiency in women worldwide. And iron deficiency is silent: it creeps up gradually, stealing your energy, concentration, and mood before you even notice the depletion.

When blood is lost heavily month after month, the body's iron stores (measured as ferritin) are depleted. Low ferritin causes:
- Fatigue that doesn't lift with rest
- Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
- Breathlessness on exertion
- Restless legs at night
- Worsening of mood and anxiety

Many women in perimenopause have their heavy periods attributed to hormones, receive no iron testing, and are left struggling with symptoms that are largely anaemia-related rather than directly hormonal.

Ask your doctor for a **ferritin test** — not just a haemoglobin test. Haemoglobin can be normal while ferritin is dangerously low. A ferritin level below 30 ng/mL is low enough to cause symptoms in many women; optimal is typically above 70 to 80 ng/mL.

**Iron-rich Indian foods to prioritise:**
- Ragi (finger millet): one of the richest plant sources of iron and calcium
- Methi (fenugreek leaves): high in iron
- Dark leafy greens: palak, drumstick leaves, amaranth
- Dates and jaggery: good sources of non-haem iron
- Meat (particularly red meat or organ meat like liver): the most bioavailable form of iron
- Beans and lentils: pair with vitamin C (lime juice, tomatoes) to improve absorption

## What Helps With Heavy Perimenopausal Bleeding?

**Dietary and lifestyle support** — as described above for iron — is foundational but not always sufficient.

**Medical options** (no specific brand names): A gynaecologist can discuss several approaches depending on your situation and whether contraception is also a goal. These include hormonal options that reduce the lining buildup, non-hormonal medications that reduce blood loss during periods, and, for confirmed fibroids or other structural causes, targeted procedures. The right choice depends on your full clinical picture.

**What doesn't help**: Waiting it out while becoming progressively anaemic. If your periods are regularly very heavy, get assessed — not only to manage the bleeding but to protect your iron stores.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I've always had heavy periods. How do I know if this is perimenopause or just how I am?**
If your periods have become heavier in recent years — particularly if accompanied by other perimenopausal symptoms like sleep disruption, worsening PMS, or cycle changes — this is likely perimenopausal progression rather than your baseline. A gynaecology review with an ultrasound and blood tests is the way to establish what's contributing.

**2. Should I be worried about blood clots during my period?**
Most clots during perimenopause are the result of anovulatory cycles and oestrogen dominance — not a bleeding disorder or cancer. However, very large clots, extremely heavy flow, or bleeding accompanied by pain, pressure, or post-coital spotting warrant a gynaecology assessment to rule out structural causes like fibroids or polyps.

**3. Could the heavy bleeding be a sign of uterine cancer?**
Uterine cancer is rare, but unexplained heavy bleeding in perimenopause — particularly if accompanied by bleeding between periods or post-coital bleeding — should be evaluated. A gynaecologist can assess the uterine lining with an ultrasound or, if indicated, a biopsy. Most women with heavy perimenopausal bleeding do not have cancer, but the question is worth asking properly.

**4. My doctor suggested a hysterectomy. Is this my only option?**
Hysterectomy is one option, but it is not the only one. Less invasive approaches — including medications that reduce lining buildup, a hormonal intrauterine device, or, for fibroids, targeted procedures — are often effective and should be discussed before major surgery. Seek a second opinion if you feel the conversation has skipped straight to surgery.

**5. My anaemia symptoms are severe. What should I do?**
If you are experiencing significant breathlessness, heart palpitations, dizziness, or inability to function normally, see a doctor promptly. Severe anaemia can require intravenous iron infusion or, in extreme cases, a transfusion — not just dietary changes. Don't delay if your symptoms are affecting your ability to function.
