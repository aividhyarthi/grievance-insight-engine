She has been tracking her cycles for two years. Every month, a bleed arrives — sometimes on time, sometimes a week late, but it comes. She assumes she is still ovulating. What she doesn't know is that in the last six months, more of her cycles have been anovulatory — bleeding without egg release — and this single change is driving the heaviest, most unpredictable periods she has ever experienced, along with anxiety, poor sleep, and a cluster of symptoms that no one has yet connected to a cause.

Anovulation is one of the most important — and most overlooked — aspects of perimenopause. Understanding it explains much of what happens in the perimenopausal body: the heavy bleeding, the mood disruption, the changing fertility, and the hormonal imbalances that underpin dozens of symptoms. And yet most women go through this entire process without ever hearing the word.

## What Is Anovulation?

Ovulation is the release of a mature egg from the ovary, triggered by a surge in LH (luteinising hormone). In a healthy ovulatory cycle, ovulation triggers the formation of the corpus luteum — the temporary glandular structure that produces progesterone for the second half of the cycle.

Anovulation means the ovary does not release an egg in a given cycle. The follicle — the structure containing the egg — may develop partially but not fully rupture. Oestrogen is still produced (sometimes in excess), and a uterine lining still builds. But without ovulation, there is no corpus luteum, and therefore no progesterone.

The bleed that follows an anovulatory cycle is not technically a "period" in the classical sense — it is an oestrogen withdrawal bleed, caused by the eventual drop in oestrogen levels. From the outside, it can look identical to a regular period, and is often heavier.

## Why Does Perimenopause Cause Anovulation?

As the ovarian reserve declines with age, the ovaries become less responsive to FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) — the hormone that drives follicle development. The pituitary gland responds by producing more FSH, trying harder to stimulate a response.

This erratic FSH signalling, combined with declining egg quality and reduced ovarian reserve, means that follicle development becomes increasingly unpredictable. Some cycles proceed to ovulation normally. Others produce a follicle that partially develops, produces oestrogen, then fails to release an egg. The ratio of anovulatory to ovulatory cycles increases steadily as perimenopause progresses.

By the final years before menopause, the majority of cycles may be anovulatory.

## What Does Anovulation Do to the Body?

The consequences of anovulatory cycles cascade across multiple body systems:

**Heavy periods**: No progesterone to oppose oestrogen means the uterine lining continues building beyond its usual thickness. When it sheds, the bleed is heavier, longer, and more likely to include clots. This is oestrogen dominance in action.

**Cycle irregularity**: Without the reliable hormonal signal of ovulation and the subsequent progesterone drop, cycles can become longer, shorter, or completely unpredictable.

**Sleep disruption**: Progesterone's sleep-supporting effect through the GABA system disappears. The 2 to 4am waking pattern is directly linked to anovulatory cycles.

**Worsening mood and anxiety**: The allopregnanolone pathway — the brain's progesterone-derived calming system — is depleted. Anxiety, irritability, and a lowered stress threshold follow.

**Breast tenderness**: Unopposed oestrogen increases breast tissue proliferation.

**Bone health impact**: Progesterone, alongside oestrogen, plays a role in bone formation. Anovulatory cycles reduce progesterone exposure and may contribute to bone density loss even before oestrogen begins its steeper decline.

## How Do You Know If You're Having Anovulatory Cycles?

The challenge is that anovulatory cycles often feel like regular cycles. There is usually still bleeding, and cycle length may still look roughly normal. But there are signs:

**Absence of mid-cycle signs**: Ovulation is normally accompanied by a clear, stretchy cervical mucus (sometimes called "egg white" mucus), occasional mid-cycle pelvic discomfort (mittelschmerz), and a slight rise in basal body temperature. If you're tracking and consistently not seeing these signs, anovulation is possible.

**Very heavy periods**: As described above — heavy, clotty, prolonged bleeding after what looked like a normal cycle.

**Abnormal cycle lengths**: Both very short cycles (under 21 days) and very long cycles (over 35 days) may indicate anovulation.

**Absence of premenstrual symptoms**: In an ovulatory cycle, progesterone in the luteal phase produces recognisable premenstrual changes (some positive, some less so). A cycle without any luteal-phase symptoms — no breast tenderness, no mood shift — may have been anovulatory.

**Confirmation tests:**
- **Day 21 progesterone**: Measured seven days before expected period. A result below 30 nmol/L suggests anovulation or inadequate luteal function.
- **LH tracking**: An LH surge (identifiable with ovulation predictor kits) confirms that ovulation was triggered. No surge suggests anovulation.
- **Basal body temperature**: A clear post-ovulatory temperature rise confirms ovulation. Absence of this pattern across multiple cycles suggests anovulation.

## What About AMH — Doesn't That Tell You About Ovulation?

This is a common misconception worth addressing. AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone) is a measure of ovarian reserve — the number of remaining follicles — not of ovulation in any given cycle. A low AMH means fewer eggs remain. It does not tell you whether you are ovulating in a current cycle.

AMH is useful for fertility assessment and for understanding ovarian reserve. It is not useful for diagnosing or monitoring anovulation in perimenopause.

## The Fertility Implications

Anovulation significantly reduces the likelihood of conception — you cannot conceive in a cycle where no egg is released. But it does not eliminate it. Anovulatory cycles and ovulatory cycles can alternate unpredictably in perimenopause. A woman can have several anovulatory cycles and then an ovulatory one. This is why fertility is reduced but not zero in perimenopause, and why unplanned pregnancy remains possible until twelve consecutive months without a period have passed (the definition of menopause).

If you are in perimenopause and do not wish to conceive, contraception remains necessary until menopause is confirmed.

## What Can Be Done?

Anovulation in perimenopause cannot be "fixed" — it is a natural part of the ovarian ageing process. But its consequences can be managed:

- Heavy, anovulatory bleeding can be addressed with hormonal or non-hormonal approaches that regulate the lining buildup — discuss options with your gynaecologist
- Sleep and mood consequences of low progesterone can be addressed through lifestyle measures and, for women who need it, medical support
- Iron levels should be monitored if heavy periods are frequent — ferritin testing is essential
- Tracking helps identify the proportion of anovulatory cycles and the pattern of symptoms

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can I still get pregnant if I'm having anovulatory cycles?**
Only in cycles where ovulation does occur. Anovulatory cycles themselves do not permit conception. But because ovulatory and anovulatory cycles can alternate unpredictably, fertility is reduced rather than absent during perimenopause. Contraception is needed until twelve consecutive period-free months confirm menopause.

**2. Does anovulation definitely mean I'm in perimenopause?**
Not necessarily. Occasional anovulatory cycles occur throughout the reproductive years — illness, stress, very high or low body weight, PCOS, and intense exercise can all cause anovulation. Perimenopause-related anovulation is distinguished by age (typically 38 and above), associated hormonal changes, and the absence of other explanations.

**3. My period is very heavy but my doctor says my hormones are fine. Could I still be having anovulatory cycles?**
A single hormonal test taken at the wrong point in the cycle can miss anovulation. If your doctor has not tested Day 21 progesterone (seven days before your expected period), that specific check has not been done. Ask for it specifically.

**4. How does anovulation affect my bones?**
Progesterone has a direct role in bone formation — it stimulates osteoblasts (bone-building cells). Anovulatory cycles mean repeated cycles without progesterone exposure, which may contribute to earlier bone density loss alongside the oestrogen decline of later perimenopause. This is another reason why adequate calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise are particularly important during this phase.

**5. I've been told I have PCOS. How does anovulation in PCOS differ from perimenopause?**
Both involve anovulation, but for different reasons. In PCOS, anovulation is driven by high androgens and insulin dysregulation that disrupt the LH surge. In perimenopause, it is driven by declining ovarian reserve and erratic FSH signalling. The hormonal context — AMH, FSH, oestradiol, androgens — will differentiate them. Both need proper evaluation.
