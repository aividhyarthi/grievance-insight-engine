She started buying pads in bulk at 43. By 45, she was doubling up. The periods that used to arrive on a Tuesday and leave by Saturday now began on a Thursday, were catastrophic by Saturday, and were still going the following Thursday. She was exhausted all the time — not just tired, but the particular heaviness of someone whose body has been losing blood repeatedly for years. She assumed this was just how perimenopause was, and that there was nothing to do but wait it out.

She was wrong on both counts. Heavy periods in perimenopause are not inevitable for everyone, are not unaddressable, and — crucially — should not be endured in silence because of the real physical toll they take. Understanding how long heavy periods typically last, what drives them, and when to get help makes an enormous difference to quality of life.

## Why Do Periods Get Heavier in Perimenopause?

The mechanism is anovulation and its consequence — oestrogen dominance. During perimenopause, an increasing proportion of cycles become anovulatory: the ovaries produce oestrogen and the uterine lining builds up, but no egg is released, no corpus luteum forms, and no progesterone is produced.

Progesterone normally opposes oestrogen's proliferative effect on the uterine lining — it signals the lining to stop thickening and to stabilise. Without it, the lining builds beyond its usual thickness. When it eventually sheds (as an oestrogen withdrawal bleed rather than a progesterone-triggered true period), it sheds heavily, with clots, and over a longer duration.

This is why heavy perimenopausal bleeding is often described as "oestrogen dominance" bleeding — not necessarily because oestrogen levels are exceptionally high, but because progesterone is too low to balance oestrogen's effect on the uterine lining.

## What Does "Heavy" Actually Mean?

Heavy bleeding has a clinical definition that many women are surprised by — because it can differ significantly from what they've normalised over years:

**Medically significant heavy bleeding:**
- Soaking a fully saturated pad or tampon every one to two hours for two or more consecutive hours
- Passing clots larger than a two-rupee coin consistently
- Periods lasting longer than seven days
- Needing to change protection during the night
- Bleeding that is severe enough to interfere with daily activities

Many women have been managing what qualifies as medically heavy bleeding for years without seeking assessment, often because they assume it's normal for perimenopause, or because there is a cultural tendency to endure menstrual problems rather than discuss them.

## How Long Do Heavy Perimenopausal Periods Typically Last?

This is the question most women want answered, and the honest answer is: it varies, but there are typical patterns.

**General timeline:**
- Heavy periods are most common in the two to four years before the final period — the late perimenopausal phase
- Bleeding tends to peak in intensity in the final eighteen months before the last period
- As cycles begin to space out (becoming every six to eight weeks rather than monthly), the individual bleeds may become lighter, as less lining has built up between them
- In the final months before periods stop, many women have a last run of irregular, sometimes lighter bleeds before the cessation

**The important caveat:** Heavy periods can persist throughout perimenopause and even until periods stop entirely. There is no reliable guarantee of improvement before menopause. For some women, bleeding becomes progressively heavier right until the end of the perimenopausal phase.

This is why waiting it out without iron monitoring and without medical support when needed is genuinely problematic.

## The Iron Deficiency Problem

Heavy periods and iron deficiency anaemia are a package that is extremely common among perimenopausal Indian women — and extremely undertreated.

Iron is essential for haemoglobin production, energy metabolism, brain function, and immune health. Repeated heavy blood loss depletes iron stores first (measurable as ferritin) and then haemoglobin itself. By the time haemoglobin drops, the iron deficiency has often been present for months.

**Symptoms of iron deficiency anaemia:**
- Persistent fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
- Breathlessness on exertion — climbing stairs, walking fast
- Brain fog, poor concentration, forgetfulness
- Pallor — pale gums, pale lower eyelids, pale nail beds
- Restless legs at night
- Feeling cold, particularly in hands and feet
- Palpitations or awareness of a fast heartbeat

If you're exhausted in perimenopause, it is very worth checking whether iron deficiency is a significant contributor. Ask specifically for **ferritin** — not just a complete blood count or haemoglobin. Ferritin is the storage form of iron and is more sensitive to depletion than haemoglobin, which can remain normal while ferritin is already critically low. A ferritin below 30 ng/mL causes symptoms in many women; below 20 ng/mL is clearly deficient.

**Iron-rich Indian foods to prioritise:**
- **Ragi (finger millet)**: Outstanding iron and calcium content. Ragi mudde, ragi rotis, ragi porridge — all excellent
- **Methi (fenugreek leaves)**: High in iron and folic acid
- **Dark leafy greens**: Palak, drumstick leaves (moringa), amaranth
- **Dates and jaggery**: Traditional iron sources; easier to absorb than plant sources but better than nothing
- **Rajma, chana, and lentils**: Pair with tomato or lime juice — the vitamin C significantly improves non-haem iron absorption
- **Meat and liver**: The most bioavailable form of iron (haem iron); even two to three servings per week makes a difference

Avoid drinking tea or coffee immediately after iron-rich meals — tannins significantly inhibit iron absorption.

## When Should Heavy Periods Be Investigated?

Heavy perimenopausal bleeding is common, but it should not be accepted without investigation — because other conditions commonly coexist and contribute:

**Fibroids**: Benign uterine tumours extremely common in Indian women in their 40s, often growing under the oestrogen-dominant perimenopausal environment. Identifiable by ultrasound.

**Adenomyosis**: Uterine lining growing into the muscle wall. Causes heavy, painful, prolonged periods. Diagnosed on ultrasound or MRI.

**Endometrial polyps**: Small outgrowths of the uterine lining. Can cause heavy and irregular bleeding. Identified on ultrasound.

**Thyroid dysfunction**: Both hypothyroidism and, occasionally, hyperthyroidism can contribute to heavy periods.

A gynaecology review for heavy perimenopausal periods should include: pelvic ultrasound (to look for structural causes), TSH, ferritin and blood count, and a consideration of endometrial sampling if indicated (particularly if over 45 with irregular bleeding or post-coital bleeding).

## Medical Options

Your gynaecologist can discuss several approaches to managing heavy perimenopausal bleeding. These include hormonal options that reduce lining buildup and regulate the cycle, non-hormonal medications that reduce blood loss during periods, and for structural causes like large fibroids, targeted procedures. Specific brand names are not listed here — the right choice depends on your circumstances, other health factors, and your contraceptive needs. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. I'm 44 and my periods have been heavy for two years. Is it going to keep getting worse?**
Not necessarily — but it is also not guaranteed to improve soon. The general pattern is that heavy bleeding peaks in the final two to three years before menopause and then resolves once periods stop. However, this is not linear for everyone, and some women have sustained heavy bleeding until the last period. Managing symptoms and iron levels in the meantime is important.

**2. My period has now been going for 10 days. When should I be worried?**
Periods lasting longer than seven days consistently are worth discussing with a gynaecologist. Ten days is on the longer end and warrants a conversation, particularly if flow is heavy throughout and accompanied by other symptoms.

**3. Will iron supplements be enough to address my anaemia?**
This depends on how depleted your iron stores are and whether the heavy bleeding continues. Dietary iron alone may not be sufficient if ferritin is very low. Oral iron supplementation can help but takes weeks to rebuild stores. Very low ferritin may require IV iron infusion under medical supervision — this is faster and more effective for severe deficiency.

**4. I've been told to wait it out — will heavy periods definitely stop when I reach menopause?**
They will stop when periods stop entirely. Menopause (twelve consecutive months without a period) marks the end of bleeding. The question is how long that takes, and whether you want to manage symptoms in the meantime. You are not obliged to simply endure heavy, depleting bleeds for years — there are options worth discussing.

**5. Can I do anything at home to reduce heavy flow?**
Anti-inflammatory foods (omega-3 rich foods like flaxseed, walnuts, and fish; turmeric in cooking) may modestly reduce prostaglandin-driven flow. Adequate iron intake helps the body cope. However, if your bleeding meets the criteria for medically heavy bleeding, home measures alone are not sufficient — a gynaecology assessment is needed.
