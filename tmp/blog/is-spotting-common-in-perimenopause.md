Light spotting between periods, brown discharge, or bleeding at unexpected times in your 40s — this is one of the most common perimenopausal experiences, and one of the most unsettling if you do not know what is causing it.

In most cases, it is your hormones. Here is what is actually happening.

---

## Why does perimenopause cause spotting?

A normal menstrual cycle depends on ovulation. When you ovulate, the corpus luteum produces progesterone, which thickens and stabilises the uterine lining. At the end of the cycle, progesterone drops, the lining sheds cleanly, and you have a period.

In perimenopause, cycles without ovulation (called **anovulatory cycles**) become increasingly common. Without ovulation, there is no corpus luteum and therefore **no progesterone**. Oestrogen continues to build the lining — but with nothing to stabilise it, the lining becomes unstable and sheds in pieces at unpredictable times.

That irregular shedding is spotting.

---

## What does perimenopausal spotting look like?

It varies, but common patterns include:

- **Light pink or brown spotting** between periods
- **Breakthrough bleeding** mid-cycle
- **Very short, light periods** that start and stop quickly
- **Heavy periods** followed by months with nothing
- Spotting after exercise or sex (usually benign but worth mentioning to a doctor)

There is no single pattern — irregular is the pattern.

---

## What is normal and what is not?

Most perimenopausal spotting is benign. But some bleeding patterns should be checked promptly:

**See a gynaecologist if:**
- Bleeding is **very heavy** — soaking more than one pad per hour for several hours
- You have **bleeding after sex** regularly
- You have **bleeding after your periods have stopped for 12 months or more** (this is post-menopausal bleeding and always needs investigation)
- Spotting is **accompanied by pain, cramping, or discharge** with an unusual smell or colour
- The pattern changes **suddenly and significantly**

These symptoms are not usually signs of something serious — but they need proper assessment to rule out other causes.

---

## Other causes of spotting to know about

Not all spotting during perimenopause is purely hormonal. A gynaecologist will also consider:

- **Cervical or uterine polyps** — benign growths that can cause irregular bleeding
- **Fibroids** — common in the 40s, can cause heavy or unpredictable bleeding
- **Thyroid dysfunction** — very common in perimenopause, disrupts cycle
- **Endometrial hyperplasia** — thickening of the uterine lining due to unopposed oestrogen
- **Infection** — less common but worth ruling out

This is why irregular bleeding in your 40s deserves a proper check — not to cause alarm, but to identify the cause.

## What Indian women should know

In India, many women hesitate to report irregular bleeding to a doctor — it feels embarrassing, or they assume it is "just part of getting older." This silence is a problem.

Irregular bleeding in perimenopause is not shameful and not trivial. It is a medical symptom with known causes and effective management. Mentioning it at your next gynaecology appointment is exactly the right thing to do.

## FAQ

### Is spotting a sign of perimenopause?

Yes — spotting and irregular bleeding are among the most common signs that perimenopause is beginning. They are caused by cycles without ovulation, which become more frequent as ovarian function changes.

### Is it normal to spot every month during perimenopause?

Irregular bleeding — including monthly spotting — is common during perimenopause. If the spotting is light, not painful, and follows no pattern, it is likely hormonal. Regular spotting that is heavy or painful should be assessed by a gynaecologist.

### Should I be worried about spotting in my 40s?

Most spotting in your 40s is a normal part of the perimenopause transition. However, very heavy bleeding, bleeding after sex, or bleeding that starts after 12 months without a period should always be checked by a doctor.

### Can stress cause spotting during perimenopause?

Stress can worsen hormonal imbalance and disrupt cycles, which may contribute to spotting. However, in perimenopause the primary cause is anovulatory cycles driven by hormonal changes rather than stress alone.

### How long does irregular bleeding last in perimenopause?

It varies significantly — from a few months to several years. On average, perimenopause lasts 4 to 10 years. Irregular bleeding typically improves as you move closer to menopause, when cycles stop altogether.
