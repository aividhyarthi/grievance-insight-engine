**Key takeaways**
- Fibroids are oestrogen-dependent — they can grow and cause heavier symptoms during early perimenopause when oestrogen fluctuates at higher levels
- The good news: almost all fibroids shrink significantly after menopause, when oestrogen permanently falls
- Heavy bleeding, pelvic pressure, or pain from fibroids during perimenopause is treatable — you do not have to wait it out in silence

---

Fibroids — benign (non-cancerous) growths of the uterine muscle — are extremely common. Studies suggest that up to 70–80% of women will develop at least one fibroid by the time they reach menopause. Many never cause symptoms. But for some women, perimenopause is the time when fibroids become most disruptive.

Here is why — and what to expect.

---

## What are fibroids?

Fibroids are non-cancerous tumours that grow in or around the uterus. They range from the size of a seed to the size of an orange. Some women have one, others have several.

They are not cancer. They do not become cancer. But depending on their size and location, they can cause:

- **Heavy or prolonged periods**
- **Pelvic pressure or fullness**
- **Frequent urination** (if pressing on the bladder)
- **Lower back pain**
- **Pain during sex**
- **Bloating or a visibly enlarged abdomen**

Many women with fibroids have no symptoms at all.

---

## What happens to fibroids during perimenopause?

Fibroids are **oestrogen-dependent** — they need oestrogen to grow. This creates a particular pattern during perimenopause.

In **early perimenopause**, oestrogen does not simply fall. It fluctuates — often surging to higher-than-normal levels before eventually declining. These surges can cause existing fibroids to grow and become more symptomatic. Women who previously had small, manageable fibroids sometimes find them becoming more problematic in their 40s.

The result can be: heavier periods, more clotting, increased pelvic pressure, and worsening pain.

In **late perimenopause and after menopause**, oestrogen falls and stays low. Fibroids lose their main growth signal and begin to shrink. Most women see significant improvement after menopause — sometimes the fibroids shrink enough to become asymptomatic entirely.

---

## Why do some women not know they have fibroids?

Many fibroids cause no symptoms. A woman can have multiple small fibroids and never know. They are often found incidentally on an ultrasound done for something else.

In India, many women normalise heavy periods — assuming it is just "how things are" or a family pattern. As a result, fibroids that are causing heavy bleeding often go undiagnosed for years.

If your periods have become noticeably heavier in your 40s, an ultrasound to check for fibroids is a reasonable and simple first step.

---

## When is treatment needed?

Not all fibroids need treatment — only when they are causing symptoms that affect your quality of life. That includes:

- **Periods heavy enough to cause anaemia** — exhaustion, dizziness, very low iron
- **Pelvic pain or pressure** that is constant or severe
- **Urinary symptoms** — needing to urinate very frequently or urgently
- **Difficulty with daily activities or work** due to any of the above

If symptoms are mild and you are close to menopause, a gynaecologist may recommend waiting — knowing that the fibroids are likely to shrink on their own after menopause.

If symptoms are significant, there are effective treatments. Speak to a gynaecologist who can assess the size, location, and symptoms before recommending anything.

---

## The iron connection

Heavy periods from fibroids during perimenopause are a very common cause of **iron deficiency anaemia** in Indian women. The symptoms — exhaustion, brain fog, breathlessness — often get attributed to stress or age when the cause is actually low iron from sustained blood loss.

If you have fibroids and heavy periods, ask your doctor to check your haemoglobin and ferritin levels.

## FAQ

### Do fibroids get worse during perimenopause?

They can — especially in early perimenopause when oestrogen surges can stimulate fibroid growth. Symptoms like heavy bleeding and pelvic pressure may worsen before they improve.

### Will fibroids shrink after menopause?

Yes — in almost all cases. Fibroids are oestrogen-dependent. Once oestrogen falls permanently after menopause, fibroids lose their growth signal and typically shrink significantly, often becoming asymptomatic.

### Is heavy bleeding during perimenopause always fibroids?

Not always — heavy bleeding in perimenopause can also be caused by hormonal imbalance alone, polyps, adenomyosis, or in rare cases, other conditions. An ultrasound and gynaecologist assessment will identify the cause.

### Can fibroids during perimenopause cause anaemia?

Yes. Heavy monthly blood loss from fibroids is a leading cause of iron deficiency anaemia in women in their 40s. If you feel persistently exhausted during perimenopause, ask your doctor to check your iron and haemoglobin levels.

### Should I have fibroid surgery during perimenopause?

This depends on your symptoms, the size and location of the fibroids, and how close you are to menopause. If symptoms are manageable and menopause is approaching, many gynaecologists recommend waiting. If symptoms severely affect your quality of life, treatment options are available — discuss with your gynaecologist.
