You're 32 and your periods have been irregular for three months. You're 29 and you've started having what might be hot flashes. You're 35 and your PMS has become severe, your periods heavier, and you haven't slept well in six months.

At what point do you actually pick up the phone and make an appointment? And when you get there, how do you make sure your concerns are taken seriously?

This depends considerably on your age. The threshold for seeking prompt medical evaluation is genuinely different at 29 versus 35 versus 40 — and knowing the difference protects your health.

## If You Are 29 or 30 With These Symptoms

Any of the following in a woman under 30 warrants prompt medical evaluation — ideally within a few weeks, not months:

- **Periods that have stopped or become very irregular** (fewer than 4 periods in the past year)
- **Hot flashes or night sweats** — vasomotor symptoms in a 29-year-old are not a minor nuisance, they are a diagnostic signal
- **Significant difficulty conceiving** (after 6 months of trying at this age)
- **Vaginal dryness** that is new and persistent
- **Mood changes, anxiety, or depression** that feel hormone-related and coincide with cycle changes

Why this urgency matters: if the ovaries are failing to function normally before age 40, this is called premature ovarian insufficiency (POI). POI has significant long-term implications for bone health and cardiovascular health because oestrogen protects both. Early diagnosis means earlier treatment that protects you from these long-term consequences.

POI does not mean permanent infertility — the ovaries still function intermittently in most cases — but it does require assessment, monitoring, and usually hormonal support.

Do not wait to "see if things settle down" if you are under 30 with cycle irregularity and hormonal symptoms. See a gynaecologist within a few weeks.

## If You Are 30 to 35 With These Symptoms

The urgency is similar. Any of the following warrants evaluation:

- **Periods stopping or occurring fewer than 6 times in the past year** — even if some return, this needs investigation
- **Clear menopausal symptoms** (hot flashes, night sweats, vaginal dryness) in a woman in her early 30s
- **Elevated FSH on a blood test** ordered for another reason

For women aged 30 to 35 with symptoms that are more subtle — worsening PMS, heavier periods, sleep disruption, new anxiety — the urgency is lower but the visit is still worth having within the next 1 to 3 months if:

- Symptoms have been present for at least 3 months
- They are significantly affecting your daily life
- Standard blood tests (thyroid, ferritin, B12) have come back normal and symptoms persist

## If You Are 35 With These Symptoms

At 35, the picture is somewhat more nuanced. Perimenopause can begin in the mid-to-late 30s, but the symptoms are often subtler and more easily attributed to other causes.

**See a gynaecologist within the next month if:**
- Your cycles have become very irregular (varying by more than 7 to 8 days regularly)
- You are having hot flashes or night sweats
- Periods have become very heavy (soaking pads hourly) or are significantly disrupting your life
- You have been trying to conceive for 6 months without success (at 35, fertility evaluation is recommended after 6 months, not 12)

**See a gynaecologist within 2 to 3 months if:**
- Worsening PMS, mood changes, sleep disruption, and brain fog have been building for 3 or more months
- Basic blood tests are normal and symptoms persist
- The symptoms are affecting your relationships or ability to work

## Red Flags at Any Age: See a Gynaecologist Urgently

Regardless of your age, certain symptoms warrant prompt evaluation — within a week:

- **Soaking a pad or tampon every hour for 2 or more consecutive hours** — this is clinically significant heavy bleeding
- **Passing clots consistently larger than a 50-paisa coin**
- **New severe depression or suicidal thoughts** — for mental health emergencies, call iCall on 9152987821 or emergency services on 112
- **Sudden severe abdominal or pelvic pain** alongside bleeding

## What to Say When You See a Gynaecologist

Many women leave consultations without having their concerns properly addressed — often because the conversation does not happen. Here is how to make the most of your appointment:

**Before you go:**
- Write a symptom list: what, when it started, how severe, how it correlates with your cycle
- Note your cycle history: average length, regularity, flow, any changes
- List what you have already had tested

**In the appointment, ask:**
- "Could early perimenopause or hormonal changes be contributing to these symptoms?"
- "Can I have FSH, LH, oestradiol, thyroid function, ferritin, B12, and vitamin D tested?"
- "What would indicate that I need to come back sooner?"

**If dismissed:** 
Some doctors will tell you that you are "too young" for perimenopause. This is not always incorrect — but it is not always a sufficient answer either. If you feel your concerns have not been properly evaluated, it is reasonable to seek a second opinion or to find a gynaecologist who has a specific interest in women's hormonal health.

> "I was 34 when I started having irregular periods and what I now know were hot flashes. My first gynaecologist told me it was 'just stress' and tested my thyroid. My second, at a women's health clinic in Bangalore, actually asked about my family history, ran FSH tests, and explained premature ovarian insufficiency to me. The first year after diagnosis was hard, but knowing what was happening was infinitely better than the confusion before."
> — Nandini, 37, Bangalore

## Finding a Gynaecologist in India Who Understands Hormonal Health

Access to gynaecologists with specific expertise in perimenopausal and reproductive hormone health varies significantly across India. In major cities, hospital-based menopause clinics exist in some institutions. Private gynaecologists who have attended specialist training in menopause management are available, though not always easy to find.

When searching, ask specifically: "Does this gynaecologist have experience with perimenopause management or early-onset hormonal issues?" Online communities and patient networks can sometimes help identify names.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I see a doctor about perimenopause at 29?**
Yes — if you are 29 with irregular or absent periods, hot flashes, night sweats, or difficulty conceiving, see a gynaecologist promptly (within a few weeks). This could indicate premature ovarian insufficiency (POI), which has significant long-term health implications and requires early management. Do not wait to see if symptoms resolve.

**What will a gynaecologist test for at age 30 with these symptoms?**
A thorough evaluation should include: FSH (measured twice, at least 4 weeks apart), LH, oestradiol, thyroid function (TSH, free T3, T4), prolactin, anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH), full blood count, ferritin, B12, and vitamin D. Chromosomal testing (fragile X premutation) may also be relevant if POI is confirmed.

**Can a gynaecologist tell if I am in perimenopause at 35?**
A gynaecologist can evaluate whether your symptom pattern is consistent with early perimenopause or another hormonal condition. Blood tests can provide supporting information, though hormone levels fluctuate widely during perimenopause and a single normal result does not rule it out. A thorough symptom history alongside blood results is most informative.

**What if my doctor says I'm too young for perimenopause?**
Early perimenopause in the late 30s is under-recognised in Indian medical practice. If you are being dismissed without proper investigation, ask specifically for FSH, LH, and oestradiol alongside thyroid, ferritin, and B12 tests. If you still feel unheard, seek a second opinion from a gynaecologist with a specific interest in hormonal health.

**What is the difference between early perimenopause and premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)?**
POI is defined as ovarian dysfunction before age 40 — typically involving very irregular or absent periods, significantly elevated FSH (above 40 IU/L on two tests), and menopausal symptoms. Early perimenopause in the late 30s typically involves still-regular cycles with worsening hormonal symptoms and more modestly elevated FSH. The distinction matters for management and for understanding long-term health implications.
