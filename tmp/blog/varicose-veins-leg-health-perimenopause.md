If you have noticed new varicose veins, visible spider veins, or legs that feel heavy, achy, or swollen by the end of the day in a way they did not before, perimenopause may be part of the reason. Vein health is genuinely affected by hormonal changes, and this stage of life is when many women notice existing veins worsening or new ones appearing for the first time.

## Why Veins Change in Perimenopause

**Oestrogen affects the strength of vein walls.** Oestrogen supports the elasticity and tone of blood vessel walls, including the veins in your legs. As oestrogen declines and fluctuates in perimenopause, vein walls can lose some of their elasticity and become more prone to stretching and the valve dysfunction that leads to varicose veins.

**Progesterone relaxes vein walls too.** Progesterone has a relaxing effect on smooth muscle throughout the body, including in vein walls. As it rises and falls unpredictably in perimenopause, this can contribute to veins becoming more prone to widening and pooling of blood, particularly in the legs.

**Weight and circulation changes compound it.** Weight gain, which is common in perimenopause, adds pressure on the veins of the legs, and reduced activity, if fatigue or joint aches limit movement, can further slow circulation and worsen venous symptoms.

## What This Looks Like

Common leg changes in perimenopause include visible varicose veins (enlarged, twisted, often bluish veins, usually on the legs), spider veins (smaller, web-like red or blue veins closer to the skin surface), legs that feel heavy, achy, or tired, especially by evening or after long periods of standing or sitting, mild swelling around the ankles that eases overnight, and occasional cramping, particularly at night.

## What Helps

**Move regularly.** This is one of the most effective and simplest interventions. Walking and regular movement activate the calf muscles, which act as a pump helping blood flow back up from the legs toward the heart. If your day involves long periods of sitting or standing, taking regular breaks to walk or move makes a real difference.

**Elevate your legs when resting.** Propping your legs up above heart level for a period each day, even fifteen to twenty minutes, helps reduce pooling and swelling, and many women find real relief from a simple daily habit like this.

**Consider compression stockings.** Graduated compression stockings support vein function and reduce heaviness, swelling, and aching, particularly useful for women who stand or sit for long periods at work. They come in various strengths, and a pharmacist or doctor can advise on the right level for you.

**Avoid prolonged sitting or standing without breaks.** If your work involves long stretches of either, build in movement breaks where possible, even brief ones, to keep circulation active.

**Maintain a healthy weight where possible.** Reducing pressure on leg veins is one of several benefits of the general weight management strategies covered elsewhere, strength training, steady blood sugar, and adequate protein.

**Stay hydrated.** Good hydration supports healthy blood volume and circulation generally.

**Avoid very tight clothing around the waist or legs**, other than properly fitted compression wear, as this can restrict circulation rather than support it.

**Consider the hormonal angle.** For some women, vein symptoms are part of a broader picture that improves somewhat with HRT, though this is not typically a primary reason to start it and is best discussed as part of your overall symptom picture with your gynaecologist.

## When to See a Doctor

**Routine appointment** if varicose veins or leg symptoms are new, bothersome, or affecting your comfort, so they can be assessed and management, including compression stockings or, for more significant veins, procedures to close or remove them, can be discussed. Varicose vein treatments have advanced considerably and many modern options are minimally invasive.

**Promptly** if you notice **sudden swelling in one leg, particularly with pain, warmth, or redness**, as this can be a sign of a blood clot (deep vein thrombosis) and needs urgent assessment, do not wait to see if it settles. Also see a doctor promptly for **skin changes near a varicose vein, such as darkening, hardening, or a sore that does not heal**, or for **bleeding from a varicose vein**, all of which need proper evaluation.

Varicose veins and heavy, achy legs are a genuine, hormone-linked part of perimenopause for many women, not simply an inevitable consequence of standing too much. Regular movement, elevation, and, where needed, compression or medical treatment can meaningfully ease symptoms and protect your leg health for the years ahead.
