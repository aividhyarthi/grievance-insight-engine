You still get your period — sometimes it comes every 25 days, sometimes 45 days, sometimes 30. But it comes. Surely that means ovulation is happening and everything is functioning normally?

Not necessarily. One of the least discussed but most consequential aspects of perimenopause is **anovulation** — cycles where bleeding occurs but no egg is released. Understanding why this happens, and what it means for your body, changes how you understand almost everything that happens during perimenopause.

## What Exactly Is Anovulation?

In a typical menstrual cycle, the brain's pituitary gland releases follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which causes a follicle in the ovary to grow and mature. As the follicle grows, it produces oestrogen. At a certain point, a surge of luteinising hormone (LH) triggers the release of the mature egg — ovulation.

Anovulation is when this process fails to complete. The follicle may begin to develop. Oestrogen may rise. But the precise hormonal timing required for the LH surge and egg release breaks down. No egg is released. A period still occurs — triggered by oestrogen withdrawal — but the defining event of the cycle, ovulation, never happened.

## Why Perimenopause Directly Causes Anovulation

As women enter perimenopause, the ovarian reserve — the pool of remaining follicles — diminishes in both quantity and quality. The remaining follicles become less responsive to FSH signals from the brain. The brain compensates by producing more FSH (which is why elevated FSH is a marker of perimenopause). But the communication between the brain and the ovaries becomes increasingly erratic.

Some cycles the signalling works. Some cycles it does not. This is why perimenopausal cycles become so unpredictable: a regular 28-day cycle one month, an anovulatory cycle the next, then a 45-day cycle after that. **Anovulatory cycles become progressively more common as perimenopause advances.**

## Why Anovulation Explains Heavier Periods

This is where the practical consequences become clear. When ovulation does occur, the empty follicle left behind — called the corpus luteum — produces progesterone. Progesterone's job is to:

1. Regulate the uterine lining, limiting how thick it grows
2. Prepare the lining for possible implantation
3. If pregnancy does not occur, orchestrate a controlled, complete shed of the lining

When ovulation does not occur, there is no corpus luteum and no progesterone. Oestrogen continues stimulating the uterine lining unoppposed. The lining grows thicker and more irregular than usual. When it eventually sheds — often after an extended or irregular cycle — it does so incompletely and heavily, producing:

- Heavier than usual bleeding
- Larger blood clots
- Longer periods
- Irregular cycle lengths

This mechanism — oestrogen without progesterone — is called **oestrogen dominance**, and anovulation is its direct cause during perimenopause.

## How Can You Tell if You Are Having Anovulatory Cycles?

Certain signs suggest a cycle may have been anovulatory:

- **No mid-cycle signs**: In an ovulatory cycle, cervical mucus typically becomes clear, stretchy, and slippery around ovulation (the "egg white" consistency). This sign is absent in anovulatory cycles
- **No mid-cycle discomfort**: Mild one-sided pelvic pain (mittelschmerz) at ovulation is absent
- **Abnormal cycle lengths**: Very short (under 21 days) or very long (over 35 days) cycles are more likely to be anovulatory
- **Very heavy periods**: Often a consequence of anovulatory cycles

The only ways to confirm ovulation actually occurred:
- **Day 21 progesterone blood test** (or 7 days after suspected ovulation): A level above 30 nmol/L suggests ovulation occurred
- **Serial LH testing** using ovulation predictor kits: Confirms whether the LH surge occurred
- **Basal body temperature tracking**: Temperature typically rises slightly (0.2–0.5°C) after ovulation and stays elevated until the next period

## What Anovulation Means for Fertility

Women in perimenopause are sometimes told they "can't get pregnant anymore." This is not reliably true on a cycle-by-cycle basis.

Anovulatory cycles significantly reduce fertility — but in any given cycle, ovulation might still occur. Women in perimenopause can and do conceive unexpectedly. Contraception remains relevant for women who wish to avoid pregnancy, up until 12 consecutive months without a period (if over 50) or 24 months (if under 50).

A common misconception: testing AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone) or antral follicle count (AFC) shows whether you ovulated. These tests measure ovarian reserve — how many follicles remain — not whether ovulation occurred in a specific cycle. They are not ovulation tests.

## What Anovulation Means for Bone Health

This is underappreciated: anovulatory cycles mean no progesterone production. Progesterone plays a role in supporting osteoblasts (the cells that build bone). Consistent anovulation — particularly if it begins in the early perimenopause and extends for years — contributes to bone loss alongside the oestrogen decline of later menopause.

This is one more reason why the hormonal health of the perimenopausal transition, including progesterone levels, matters beyond symptom management.

> "I had my AMH tested at 45 and my gynaecologist told me it was still reasonable for my age, which she said meant I was 'definitely still ovulating.' I kept having very heavy periods — soaking protection in two hours some months. It was only when I read about anovulation that I asked specifically for a Day 21 progesterone test. It was practically undetectable. I had been having anovulatory cycles for who knows how long, but nobody had checked."
> — Vandana, 48, Kochi

## What Can Help When Anovulation Is Causing Problems?

If anovulatory heavy bleeding is significantly affecting your life, a gynaecologist can discuss options. General approaches include:

- Identifying and addressing underlying drivers (thyroid function, for example, directly affects ovulatory function and should be checked)
- Hormonal approaches to regulate the uterine lining — a gynaecologist can explain options appropriate to your specific situation
- Dietary support for iron replenishment if anaemia has developed from heavy bleeding

Understanding that anovulation — not a uterine pathology — is the driver of the heavy bleeding is often itself helpful: it explains the mechanism and points toward hormonal rather than surgical solutions as a first step.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What does anovulation mean in simple terms?**
Anovulation means a menstrual cycle in which no egg is released from the ovaries, even though bleeding still occurs. During perimenopause, anovulatory cycles become increasingly common because the ovaries respond less reliably to hormonal signals from the brain.

**Does anovulation always cause heavy periods?**
Anovulation often causes heavier periods because without ovulation there is no progesterone production. Without progesterone to regulate the uterine lining, it builds up thicker and sheds more heavily. But anovulatory cycles can also sometimes produce very light or short periods — the pattern varies.

**How do I know if my cycle was anovulatory?**
Signs include the absence of mid-cycle cervical mucus changes, no mid-cycle discomfort, unusual cycle lengths, and very heavy periods. A blood test (mid-luteal phase progesterone, Day 21 in a 28-day cycle) is the most accessible way to confirm whether ovulation occurred. If the result is very low, ovulation likely did not happen that cycle.

**Can I still get pregnant during perimenopause if I have anovulatory cycles?**
Yes, potentially. Anovulatory cycles are interspersed with ovulatory cycles in perimenopause — any given cycle could be either. Contraception is still relevant for women who do not wish to conceive, until 12 months (over 50) or 24 months (under 50) of no periods have passed.

**Is anovulation during perimenopause permanent?**
No. During perimenopause, ovulatory and anovulatory cycles alternate unpredictably. It is only after the final menstrual period — confirmed after 12 months with no bleeding — that ovulation has permanently stopped.
