You are 44, you have a demanding job, and the last six months have been unlike anything you have experienced before. You are forgetting things you should not forget. You have had to excuse yourself from a meeting twice because of a sudden wave of heat that left you drenched. Your concentration in the afternoon has become unreliable. You are arriving at work having slept four or five broken hours, and you are managing it — barely.

Most of the women around you are managing something similar. None of them are talking about it.

Perimenopause in the workplace is one of the most pervasive and least discussed health challenges facing India's working women. Here is how to recognise it, understand it, and practically manage it.

## How Perimenopause Shows Up at Work

### Brain Fog During Presentations and Meetings

Forgetting words mid-sentence in a presentation. Losing track of a point you were making. Finding that complex analysis takes significantly longer than it used to. This cognitive haziness — driven by oestrogen's role in memory, verbal fluency, and processing speed — is one of the most professionally alarming perimenopause symptoms.

It typically affects verbal memory (remembering what was said, finding words) more than written or visual processing. Many women find that writing things down and structuring their thinking more explicitly helps compensate during this phase.

### Anxiety Before — and During — Meetings and Presentations

Sudden surges of anxiety that seem disconnected from your usual relationship with your work. Racing heart before a meeting. Disproportionate dread before a client call you have made hundreds of times before. This perimenopausal anxiety is neurological, not psychological — it reflects oestrogen's role in regulating the nervous system.

In a work context, it can lead women to avoid situations they previously navigated confidently, which then compounds the professional impact.

### Managing Heavy Periods at Work

Needing to change protection every hour during the heaviest days. The logistics of managing flooding or unexpected heavy bleeding in an office environment. The planning required around the first two days of a period — scheduling meetings differently, ensuring you have what you need.

This is the aspect of perimenopausal experience most likely to cause absence from work. Yet it is almost never discussed as an occupational health matter in India.

### Sleep Deprivation and Its Cascading Effects

Working on four to five hours of fragmented sleep, night after night, has measurable effects on cognitive performance, emotional regulation, and decision-making. Chronic sleep deprivation from perimenopausal sleep disruption looks indistinguishable in the workplace from disengagement, low motivation, or diminished capability.

Many women are managing significant sleep deficits while maintaining professional performance — often at considerable personal cost.

### Hot Flashes in Air-Conditioned Offices

The paradox: air-conditioned offices that are comfortable for most colleagues can trigger hot flashes in perimenopausal women, because the temperature contrast when moving between environments (or even the physical stillness of sitting at a desk) can initiate a hot flash. Conversely, offices that are warmer can make hot flashes worse.

Practical strategies: wearing layers that can be removed, choosing natural fibres (cotton, linen) that allow the skin to breathe, having a small desk fan available, and choosing seats near a window or air vent where temperature is adjustable.

## The "Invisible Load" at This Life Stage

For many Indian women, the late 30s and 40s represent a convergence of professional demands (peak career years, senior roles, responsibility) with intensified family demands (adolescent children, ageing parents, potentially the most complex phase of managing a household).

The cultural expectation is that women manage all of this without visible strain. Perimenopause symptoms are experienced on top of this load — and because they are often invisible (brain fog, sleep deprivation, anxiety), they are attributed to the "stress of everything" rather than recognised as a physiological change requiring attention.

This matters because the response to "stress of everything" is to push harder, rest less, and ignore symptoms. The response to perimenopause is to understand what is happening and address the underlying cause.

> "I was running a team of 18 people at 43 and having the worst brain fog I have ever experienced. I was writing everything down — I had always prided myself on my memory — and I was convinced I had early dementia. My HR manager knew I was struggling but had no framework for what was happening. When I finally understood it was perimenopause, I felt something release. It wasn't me failing — it was my hormones adjusting. That reframe changed everything about how I managed it."
> — Kavitha, 47, Bengaluru

## Practical Strategies for Each Symptom at Work

### For Brain Fog
- Keep a written list for meetings: the points you want to make, questions you want to ask
- Batch cognitively demanding work for your sharpest hours (often mornings for sleep-disrupted people)
- Minimise multitasking — single-task more deliberately
- Regular short breaks restore cognitive function better than powering through
- Reduce alcohol, which worsens brain fog significantly

### For Anxiety
- Brief breathwork before high-stakes situations: 4-count inhale, 4-count hold, 6-count exhale, repeated 5 times — activates the parasympathetic nervous system
- Physical movement in the middle of the day (even a 10-minute walk) reduces anxiety load
- Reduce caffeine, which amplifies perimenopausal anxiety significantly
- Identify the cycle pattern: if your anxiety is predictably worse in the week before your period, you can plan lighter workloads in that window

### For Heavy Periods
- Track your cycle to anticipate heavy days
- Carry a small "emergency kit" in your bag or desk
- Choose darker clothing or protect yourself with an extra layer on heavy days
- If flooding is causing missed work, this is a medical issue warranting gynaecological evaluation — not just a scheduling problem

### For Sleep Deprivation
- Prioritise sleep as a non-negotiable — later nights when sleep is already disrupted compound the problem significantly
- Keep the bedroom cool
- Limit screens after 9pm
- If you wake at 3am, a brief relaxation practice (body scan, slow breathing) is more effective than phone use

### For Hot Flashes
- Keep a desk fan
- Wear layers you can remove
- Identify your personal triggers: common ones include caffeine, spicy food, hot drinks, stress, and alcohol
- Cold water — a bottle on your desk — is useful in a flash

## The Workplace Conversation: How Much to Share

In India's corporate culture, perimenopause is rarely discussed openly. The degree to which you share is a personal decision based on your specific workplace culture, your relationship with your manager, and your role.

Some women find it helpful to have a brief conversation with a trusted manager or HR: "I have a health condition that is causing some sleep disruption and fatigue. I am managing it, but I wanted to flag it in case it affects my availability temporarily." This does not require naming perimenopause.

If your workplace has an Employee Assistance Programme (EAP), this may offer confidential support.

The goal is not to make perimenopause a workplace issue — it is to get the support you need without unnecessarily compromising your professional standing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How does perimenopause affect work performance?**
Perimenopause can affect work performance through brain fog (memory, verbal fluency, processing speed), sleep deprivation from night sweats or early morning waking, anxiety that interferes with confidence and decision-making, and the practical management of heavy periods. Most women manage effectively — but often at significant personal cost, without the support they could have.

**How do I explain perimenopause to my manager or HR?**
You do not need to use the word "perimenopause" unless you choose to. Brief framing like "I am managing a health condition that affects my sleep and energy" is accurate and sufficient for requesting flexibility. If your workplace is supportive, being more specific may allow more targeted support.

**Can perimenopause cause poor concentration at work?**
Yes. Brain fog — difficulty concentrating, word-finding difficulties, slower processing — is a recognised perimenopausal symptom directly tied to oestrogen's role in cognitive function. It is not a permanent change; it typically improves after menopause when hormone levels stabilise.

**What is the best way to manage hot flashes in an office?**
Keep a desk fan, wear moisture-wicking layers in natural fibres, carry a cold water bottle, and identify your personal triggers (caffeine, spicy food, stress, alcohol are common). Avoid polyester and synthetic fabrics. Cold water on the wrists or back of the neck can help during a flash.

**Is there any support for perimenopausal employees in India?**
Formal menopause workplace policies are rare in India, though they are being introduced in some progressive organisations. Some companies offer flexible working arrangements that can help.
