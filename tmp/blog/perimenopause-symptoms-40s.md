Your periods have been arriving a few days earlier than usual. You're waking at 3am and lying there, mind running, unable to get back to sleep. Your PMS feels worse than it did at 32. And last month, your period was so heavy you had to cancel a day of meetings. You're 43, and something has shifted — but nobody told you it would start this soon.

In India, the average age of menopause is 46 to 47 years — four to five years earlier than the Western average of 51 to 52. This means perimenopause, the transitional phase that precedes the final period, often begins in the late 30s to early 40s for Indian women. If you're 40 and noticing changes, you're not imagining things, and you're not "too young." You may be right on schedule.

## What Does Your Cycle Actually Start Doing First?

The earliest signal is usually a shortening of the cycle. Instead of a 28 to 30-day cycle, you might notice 24 to 26-day cycles for several months. This happens because declining progesterone levels shorten the luteal phase — the second half of your cycle after ovulation. Later, cycles become erratic: sometimes short, sometimes long, sometimes skipped altogether.

Heavy periods often follow. When cycles become anovulatory (no egg is released despite bleeding), there is no corpus luteum and therefore no progesterone. Oestrogen continues to build up the uterine lining unchecked, and when it finally sheds, the result is a heavy, sometimes clotty bleed. This is called oestrogen dominance, and it is one of the defining features of early perimenopause.

## Why Are You Waking Between 2 and 4am?

This one surprises almost everyone. Sleep disruption — specifically early morning waking — is one of the most consistent early symptoms of perimenopause, and it is directly linked to progesterone decline.

Progesterone acts on GABA receptors in the brain, the same receptors that govern calming and sedation. When progesterone levels drop, the calming effect on the nervous system diminishes. The result: you may fall asleep fine, but wake at 2 or 3am, mind alert, unable to settle. This is not insomnia in the conventional sense. It is a hormonal pattern, and it often precedes the hot flashes that many women associate with menopause.

Oestrogen also plays a role in regulating body temperature. As oestrogen levels fluctuate erratically during perimenopause, the hypothalamus becomes hypersensitive to small changes in body temperature, triggering night sweats that can further disrupt sleep.

## Is Your Anxiety New — or Just Much Worse?

Many women in their early 40s describe a new quality of anxiety — a jitteriness, a tendency to catastrophise, a sense of dread before situations that never bothered them before. This is not a personality change. It is, again, largely progesterone.

Progesterone's metabolite allopregnanolone directly modulates the stress response. When progesterone declines, the buffer against cortisol diminishes. Everyday stressors hit harder. Some women describe it as feeling like their nervous system is running hot all the time.

Oestrogen also affects serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine. As oestrogen levels swing unpredictably, so does mood. The result can look like depression, generalised anxiety, or simply feeling like yourself on a bad day — except it is most days now.

## When Do the Hot Flashes Begin?

Classic hot flashes — a sudden wave of heat through the chest and face, lasting 30 seconds to a few minutes — are more typical of later perimenopause and menopause itself. At 40 to 42, many women have night sweats and temperature sensitivity without full hot flashes. By 44 or 45, if cycles are becoming notably irregular, hot flashes often begin.

If you are not yet having hot flashes, this does not mean you are not in perimenopause. The earlier symptoms — cycle changes, sleep disruption, mood shifts, brain fog — arrive first.

## What About Brain Fog?

Forgetting words mid-sentence. Losing track of what you were saying in a meeting. Walking into a room and having no idea why. These cognitive symptoms are real, they are common in the early 40s, and they are directly connected to oestrogen fluctuations.

Oestrogen supports memory consolidation, verbal fluency, and cognitive processing speed. When it begins to fluctuate, these functions are affected. The good news: research consistently shows that cognitive changes are most pronounced during the perimenopausal transition and typically improve after menopause, when hormone levels stabilise at their new baseline.

## Weight Changes and Other Physical Symptoms

Unexplained weight gain — particularly around the abdomen — despite no significant changes to diet or exercise is a common complaint in the 40s. Oestrogen helps regulate fat distribution; as it fluctuates, the body tends to redistribute fat towards the midsection. Metabolic changes also slow calorie burning.

Skin changes — dryness, new breakouts, or loss of elasticity — reflect shifting hormone levels. Fatigue that is disproportionate to how much sleep you are getting is another very common experience. Joint aches, headaches, and digestive changes can also emerge.

## What Tests Are Worth Having?

If you are in your 40s and experiencing these symptoms, the following tests can be informative:

- **FSH and LH**: Elevated FSH is one indicator of declining ovarian function, though levels fluctuate widely during perimenopause
- **Oestradiol**: Provides context alongside FSH
- **Thyroid function** (TSH, free T3, free T4): Thyroid dysfunction overlaps significantly with perimenopausal symptoms and is common in Indian women
- **Full blood count, ferritin**: Rule out anaemia — very common with heavy periods
- **Vitamin D, B12**: Deficiencies worsen fatigue and mood

Important: normal blood results do not rule out perimenopause. It is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on your symptoms, age, and cycle changes.

## What Can You Do?

Lifestyle approaches are effective and well-evidenced:

- **Sleep**: Keep the bedroom cool, avoid screens after 9pm, maintain a consistent wake time — even on weekends
- **Strength training**: Supports bone density, metabolic health, and mood — all affected by declining oestrogen
- **Diet**: Phytoestrogens (soy milk, tofu, lentils, flaxseed), calcium sources (ragi, sesame, dairy), and anti-inflammatory foods support the perimenopausal body
- **Stress reduction**: Cortisol worsens oestrogen dominance symptoms — yoga, pranayama, and adequate rest directly benefit hormonal health

If symptoms are significantly affecting your quality of life, a conversation with a gynaecologist about hormonal support options is worthwhile. Help is available.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What are the most common perimenopause symptoms at 40-45?**
The most common symptoms in the early-to-mid 40s are: changes in the menstrual cycle (shorter, heavier, or more irregular periods), sleep disruption (especially waking between 2 and 4am), worsening PMS or mood changes, new or worsening anxiety, and brain fog. Hot flashes are more typical from the mid-40s onward.

**Can perimenopause start at 40?**
Yes. For Indian women, perimenopause typically begins between 40 and 45, with some women entering the transition in the late 30s. The average age of menopause in India is 46–47, which means the transition leading up to it begins earlier than many women expect.

**How do I know if it's perimenopause or something else at 40?**
Perimenopausal symptoms tend to worsen in relation to the menstrual cycle and build over months. They often coincide with changes in period pattern. **Do I need to see a gynaecologist about perimenopause at 40?**
If your symptoms are affecting your sleep, relationships, or ability to work, yes — it is worth seeking a consultation. Come with a record of your symptoms and their timing in relation to your cycle. You do not need to wait until you have hot flashes or irregular periods to seek support.

**Will perimenopause symptoms at 40 last for years?**
Perimenopause typically lasts between 4 and 10 years, though this varies. The transition is usually most active in the mid-to-late 40s. Many symptoms can be effectively managed with lifestyle changes, targeted supplements, or hormonal support — you do not have to simply endure them.
