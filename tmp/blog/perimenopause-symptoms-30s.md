You're 37, your periods have been heavier for the past few months, and you're waking up at 3am more nights than not. Your PMS has become almost unbearable — the anxiety, the irritability, the breast tenderness that starts a full week before your period. You mention it to your doctor, who runs a thyroid test. Normal. She puts it down to stress. But it doesn't feel like just stress.

Here is what is rarely said plainly: yes, perimenopause symptoms can begin in the late 30s. The hormonal changes that precede menopause are not a sudden event — they are a gradual process that can start quietly, a decade or more before the final period. For Indian women, whose average menopause age is 46 to 47, symptoms beginning at 35 to 38 are entirely plausible.

## What Is Actually Happening Hormonally in Your Late 30s?

Around the mid-to-late 30s, even before cycles become visibly irregular, the ovaries begin to produce progesterone less reliably after ovulation. Progesterone is the hormone released by the corpus luteum (the empty follicle left after an egg is released). It prepares the uterine lining for possible pregnancy and, if pregnancy doesn't occur, orchestrates a clean, controlled shed.

When progesterone production falls or becomes erratic, several things happen simultaneously:

- The luteal phase shortens, bringing your period earlier
- PMS intensifies because the progesterone buffer on the nervous system diminishes
- Sleep quality worsens, particularly in the first half of the night and again in the early hours
- The uterine lining builds up more than it should, leading to heavier periods

This phase — sometimes called the late reproductive or early menopausal transition — does not yet produce irregular cycles. You may be having periods like clockwork and still be experiencing significant hormonal shifts.

## Is This Perimenopause or Something Else?

The symptoms of early perimenopausal hormonal shifts in the late 30s overlap heavily with:

- **Thyroid dysfunction**: Very common in Indian women, causes fatigue, mood changes, irregular periods, hair loss
- **Iron deficiency anaemia**: Often caused by heavy periods, worsening a cycle of fatigue and heavier bleeding
- **Vitamin B12 or D deficiency**: Extremely common in India, causes neurological symptoms including brain fog, anxiety, and fatigue
- **PCOS or other gynaecological conditions**: Can cause hormonal imbalances at any age

This is why the sequence matters: get the basics ruled out (thyroid, ferritin, B12, vitamin D). If those tests come back normal and you still have worsening PMS, heavier periods, sleep disruption, and new anxiety — the remaining explanation is most likely hormonal, and perimenopause deserves to be part of the conversation.

## What Are the Most Common Symptoms in Your Late 30s?

### Worsening PMS

Premenstrual symptoms that have escalated from mild inconvenience to something that alters relationships, work, or sense of self is one of the most common early signals. Irritability, tearfulness, rage, and low mood in the week before your period — especially if these are more intense than they used to be — reflect erratic progesterone production.

### Heavier Periods

Periods becoming notably heavier — needing to change protection more frequently, passing larger clots, or bleeding for more days than before — often begins in the late 30s and reflects the early hormonal shifts of perimenopause.

### Sleep Changes

Difficulty maintaining sleep, particularly waking in the early hours and finding it hard to return to sleep, is a progesterone-driven symptom that frequently begins in the 30s. You may not yet have night sweats — the disruption is often "just" waking at 3am with a racing mind.

### New or Worsening Anxiety

A specific perimenopausal anxiety — characterised by sudden surges of unease, a sense of dread, difficulty settling the mind — can begin in the late 30s. It tends to be cyclic, often worsening in the week before the period, though it may eventually become more constant.

### Breast Tenderness

Breast tenderness that is more pronounced than before, often starting earlier in the cycle, reflects oestrogen surges. During early perimenopause, oestrogen doesn't simply decline — it spikes erratically before eventually falling. These spikes stimulate breast tissue.

## Is This Premature Ovarian Insufficiency?

It is important to distinguish between early perimenopause symptoms (which can occur in the late 30s while cycles are still regular) and premature ovarian insufficiency (POI).

POI is defined as ovarian dysfunction before the age of 40 — typically involving missed periods, very elevated FSH levels, and menopausal symptoms. It affects about 1% of women under 40. POI requires medical investigation and management because of its implications for bone health, cardiovascular health, and fertility.

If you are under 40 and your periods are becoming irregular or missing, this needs prompt evaluation with a gynaecologist. If you are in your late 30s with symptoms but still cycling regularly, this is more likely early perimenopausal hormonal changes and less urgently concerning — though still worth discussing.

## How Can You Track These Symptoms?

If you suspect hormonal changes are behind your symptoms, tracking in relation to your cycle is enormously useful:

- Note when symptoms begin, peak, and resolve each month
- Track your cycle length (day 1 of bleeding to day 1 of next bleed)
- Note when PMS begins (premenstrual days) and how intense it is
- Note sleep quality each night
- Note energy, mood, and anxiety levels

This pattern, documented over 2 to 3 months, gives a gynaecologist far more useful information than a one-time blood test.

## When Should You See a Gynaecologist?

In your late 30s, see a gynaecologist if:

- Periods are becoming very heavy (soaking pads hourly) or very irregular
- You have complete cycle irregularity (skipping months) under 40 — this needs urgent evaluation for POI
- Symptoms are significantly affecting your daily life, relationships, or ability to work
- Basic blood tests (thyroid, ferritin, B12, D) are normal but symptoms persist

Come with a symptom timeline. Ask specifically whether early perimenopause could be a contributing factor, and request FSH, LH, and oestradiol testing alongside the standard panel.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can perimenopause start at 35?**
Yes. While perimenopause most commonly begins between 40 and 45 for Indian women, the earliest hormonal changes — particularly declining progesterone and worsening PMS — can begin in the mid-to-late 30s. If you are under 40 with missed or very irregular periods, this needs specific evaluation for premature ovarian insufficiency.

**How do I know if my symptoms at 37 are perimenopause or just stress?**
Both cause sleep disruption, mood changes, and fatigue. The key distinction is pattern: perimenopausal symptoms typically correlate with the menstrual cycle (worst in the week before your period) and build over months. They often accompany changes in cycle length or flow. Stress symptoms tend to correlate with life circumstances. A symptom diary over 2 to 3 months can make this pattern clearer. **What blood tests should I ask for at 38 with these symptoms?**
Ask for: FSH and LH (ovarian function), oestradiol, thyroid function (TSH, free T3, free T4), full blood count, serum ferritin (iron stores), vitamin B12, vitamin D, and fasting blood glucose. Normal results for most of these alongside ongoing symptoms makes perimenopause more likely.

**Will my doctor take me seriously about perimenopause at 38?**
Some will, some won't — perimenopause in the late 30s is under-recognised in Indian medical practice. Come with a written record of your symptoms, their timing in relation to your cycle, and their impact on your life. Ask specifically: "Could early perimenopause be contributing to these symptoms?" Request the blood tests listed above.

**What can help perimenopausal symptoms in your late 30s?**
Tracking the pattern helps you understand and anticipate your cycle. Lifestyle interventions — consistent sleep, regular exercise (including strength training), reduced sugar and alcohol, and stress management practices — support progesterone-related symptoms. Some women find magnesium helpful for sleep and PMS. If symptoms are significantly affecting your life, a gynaecologist can discuss hormonal support options.
