You find a large blood clot on your pad and your stomach drops. You know, rationally, that it is probably not dangerous — but it is disturbing in a way that is hard to dismiss. And the periods have been so much heavier lately. More clots. Longer. Flooding. Your body feels unfamiliar.

You are not alone. Heavy periods with clotting are one of the most common perimenopausal experiences for Indian women in their 40s, and there is a direct hormonal explanation for why it happens.

## Why Perimenopause Causes Heavy, Clotty Periods

The mechanism comes down to one word: anovulation.

During perimenopause, cycles increasingly become anovulatory — meaning the menstrual cycle continues and bleeding occurs, but no egg is released. Without ovulation, there is no corpus luteum (the structure left behind after an egg is released), and therefore no progesterone production.

Progesterone's key role in the menstrual cycle is to limit how thick the uterine lining becomes and to orchestrate a controlled, clean shed. Without progesterone to oppose it, oestrogen continues stimulating the uterine lining, which grows thicker than it normally would.

When this thick lining finally sheds, it does so irregularly and incompletely — producing heavier bleeding, longer periods, and tissue that forms clots as it exits.

This is called **oestrogen dominance** — not because oestrogen is unusually high, but because it is operating without the counterbalance of progesterone.

## What Does a "Normal" Perimenopause Clot Look Like?

Clots during perimenopause are very common. What is generally considered within the expected range:

- Clots smaller than a 2-rupee coin
- Occurring mainly at the beginning of the period or during the heaviest flow
- Alongside heavy but not completely unmanageable bleeding

What warrants medical attention:

- **Clots larger than a 50-paisa coin consistently** across multiple cycles
- **Soaking a full-sized pad or tampon every hour for two or more consecutive hours** — this is the clinical definition of excessively heavy bleeding (menorrhagia)
- **Periods lasting more than 7 days** regularly
- **Symptoms of anaemia**: fatigue that is disproportionate to activity levels, breathlessness on mild exertion, pallor, dizziness, racing heart, difficulty concentrating

If any of the above apply, see a gynaecologist — not for reassurance, but for evaluation and management.

## What Else Could Be Causing Heavy, Clotty Periods?

Perimenopause is the most common explanation, but other conditions can cause or worsen heavy bleeding. These need to be ruled out:

**Fibroids (uterine myomas)**: Benign muscle growths in or on the uterus. They are very common in Indian women, particularly in the 40s, and are a leading cause of heavy periods. An ultrasound can detect them. Small fibroids may not cause symptoms; larger or submucosal fibroids (inside the uterine cavity) often do.

**Adenomyosis**: When the uterine lining grows into the muscle of the uterus. Causes very heavy, painful periods. More common than previously recognised, and the symptoms often worsen during perimenopause.

**Endometrial polyps**: Small growths on the uterine lining. Can cause irregular or heavy bleeding. Detected by ultrasound or hysteroscopy.

**Thyroid dysfunction**: Both under-active and over-active thyroid can disrupt the menstrual cycle and worsen bleeding. Thyroid testing (TSH, free T3, T4) is essential.

**Clotting disorders**: Rare, but conditions like von Willebrand disease can cause heavy menstrual bleeding. Worth considering if heavy periods have been a lifelong pattern.

## The Anaemia Problem

This is where blood clots become more than inconvenient. Heavy periods — month after month — deplete iron stores, often leading to iron-deficiency anaemia. Anaemia then causes:

- Extreme fatigue and weakness
- Breathlessness on mild activity
- Heart palpitations
- Difficulty concentrating (brain fog)
- Pale skin, pallor inside the eyelids
- Hair loss

Many women experiencing these symptoms are told they are "just tired" or that it is "stress." A serum ferritin test (not just haemoglobin — ferritin measures stored iron) can identify iron deficiency before it becomes severe anaemia.

## Iron-Rich Foods to Support Heavy-Period Recovery

If you are losing significant blood each month, dietary iron becomes important. Particularly good sources in the Indian diet:

- **Ragi (finger millet)**: Exceptionally rich in calcium and contains iron
- **Dates, jaggery**: Traditional iron sources, though modest
- **Dark leafy greens**: Spinach, methi (fenugreek leaves), amaranth (rajgira)
- **Lentils and legumes**: Dal, rajma, chana — paired with vitamin C (lemon, tomato) to enhance absorption
- **Meat and poultry**: Highly bioavailable iron
- **Sesame seeds (til)**: Rich in iron and calcium

Avoid drinking tea or coffee with iron-rich meals, as tannins reduce iron absorption.

## What to Ask Your Gynaecologist

If you are experiencing heavy, clotty periods, a thorough evaluation should include:

- Pelvic ultrasound (to check for fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis)
- Thyroid function panel
- Full blood count and serum ferritin
- FSH and oestradiol (to assess perimenopausal status)
- Endometrial thickness assessment (part of the pelvic ultrasound)

Based on findings, your gynaecologist can discuss management options including hormonal approaches to regulate the lining and reduce bleeding.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it normal to pass blood clots during perimenopause?**
Yes, clots during perimenopause are common and usually caused by anovulatory cycles — cycles where no egg is released and no progesterone is produced. Without progesterone to regulate the uterine lining, it builds up and sheds heavily with clots. Clots up to the size of a 2-rupee coin are common; consistently larger clots with very heavy bleeding need evaluation.

**When should I be worried about blood clots during my period?**
See a gynaecologist if you are soaking a full pad or tampon every hour for two or more consecutive hours, passing clots consistently larger than a 50-paisa coin, bleeding for more than 7 days, or experiencing symptoms of anaemia (severe fatigue, breathlessness, palpitations, dizziness).

**Can fibroids cause blood clots in periods?**
Yes. Fibroids, particularly those inside the uterine cavity (submucosal fibroids), are a significant cause of heavy, clotty periods. They are very common in Indian women in their 40s. A pelvic ultrasound can detect them. Perimenopause and fibroids often occur together and can compound each other's effects on bleeding.

**Can diet help with heavy periods and clotting?**
Diet can help manage the anaemia that results from heavy bleeding, but it will not stop the heavy bleeding itself. Eat iron-rich foods (ragi, dark leafy greens, legumes, dates) with vitamin C to maximise absorption. For the heavy bleeding, treatment depends on the cause — a gynaecologist should be consulted.

**Will the heavy periods and clots stop on their own?**
They often do — eventually. As perimenopause progresses and cycles become less frequent, heavy bleeding typically reduces. However, this can take several years, and the anaemia and practical disruption in the interim are significant. Medical management is available and effective; there is no reason to simply wait it out if the bleeding is affecting your quality of life.
