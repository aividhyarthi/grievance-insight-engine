You have been having very heavy periods for the past eight months. You are going through protection faster than you ever have. The clots are larger. The cramps are worse. And when you ask how long this will go on for, you get a vague answer that does not actually help you plan your life around it.

Here is the honest answer: heavy perimenopausal periods can last for months or years, and the duration is genuinely variable. But there are patterns, and there are things that can be done.

## Why Heavy Periods Happen in Perimenopause

The mechanism begins with anovulation — the increasing number of menstrual cycles in perimenopause where no egg is released. Without ovulation, there is no progesterone production. Without progesterone, the uterine lining builds up under oestrogen's influence without the counterbalancing effect that progesterone provides.

When this thickened, irregular lining eventually sheds, it does so incompletely and heavily. This is oestrogen dominance, and it is the direct cause of the heavy, prolonged, and sometimes flooding periods that many women experience in their 40s.

## When Does Heavy Bleeding Typically Start and Peak?

Heavy perimenopausal periods most commonly emerge in the early perimenopause — typically the 40s for Indian women — and often **peak in the 2 to 4 years before the final menstrual period**.

This is because anovulatory cycles become more and more frequent as ovarian reserves diminish. In early perimenopause, some cycles are anovulatory and some are not. As perimenopause advances, anovulatory cycles dominate.

In the 12 to 18 months before the final period, many women report some of the heaviest bleeding of their lives — partly because cycles are longer (giving oestrogen more time to build the lining) and partly because anovulation is now near-universal.

## When Are Heavy Periods Likely to Ease?

The reassuring reality: heavy periods typically **begin to ease in late perimenopause**, as cycles become less frequent and eventually stop. Once periods are skipping months rather than arriving every 3 to 4 weeks, each individual bleed often becomes lighter.

However, it is important to be honest: "late perimenopause" may be 2 to 4 or more years away when heavy bleeding first begins. And for some women, bleeding worsens right up until the final period.

Waiting it out is a valid choice — but it is not the only one, and it carries a real health cost if anaemia develops in the interim.

## What Does "Heavy" Actually Mean Medically?

The clinical definition of heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) includes:

- **Soaking a full-sized pad or tampon every hour for two or more consecutive hours**
- **Needing to wear double protection** (pad plus tampon) regularly
- **Bleeding for more than 7 days**
- **Consistently passing clots larger than a 50-paisa coin**
- **Anaemia symptoms**: extreme fatigue, breathlessness with mild activity, racing heartbeat, pallor inside the lower eyelids

Not all heavy periods meet the clinical threshold, but many women are experiencing significant disruption to their daily life well below it. Both deserve attention.

## The Anaemia Problem: Why You Cannot Simply Wait

Month after month of heavy bleeding depletes iron stores. Iron-deficiency anaemia develops progressively — often slowly enough that women adapt to its symptoms and attribute them to "just being tired."

Signs of iron-deficiency anaemia include:

- Fatigue that is disproportionate to activity
- Breathlessness or a racing heart on mild exertion
- Difficulty concentrating (worsening brain fog)
- Hair loss or increased hair shedding
- Pallor — particularly noticeable inside the lower eyelids
- Cold intolerance

A serum ferritin test (not just haemoglobin — ferritin measures stored iron and drops before haemoglobin does) is the most useful test. Even a ferritin level in the low-normal range (under 30 µg/L) can cause significant fatigue.

Heavy perimenopausal bleeding and iron-deficiency anaemia often create a cycle: anaemia causes fatigue that is attributed to the perimenopause, while the actual cause (heavy blood loss) continues unaddressed.

> "I had been having very heavy periods for nearly two years by the time I found out I was severely anaemic. I had put the exhaustion down to the perimenopause and the stress of work. My haemoglobin was very low. Once I addressed both the anaemia and the underlying hormonal cause, the difference in how I felt was remarkable — I had forgotten what normal energy felt like."
> — Geeta, 48, Ahmedabad

## Iron-Rich Foods for Recovering From Heavy Period Anaemia

While medical treatment is often needed for significant anaemia, building dietary iron intake supports recovery. Useful iron sources in the Indian diet:

- **Ragi (finger millet)**: One of the best plant sources of iron and calcium; excellent in porridge or dosa
- **Methi (fenugreek) leaves**: Rich in iron; available fresh, dried, or as seeds
- **Rajgira (amaranth)**: High in iron; used in laddoos and porridges
- **Spinach, palak**: Cooked spinach is a useful iron source (cooking reduces oxalates that inhibit absorption)
- **Dates and jaggery**: Traditional sources, though modest in quantity
- **Chicken and red meat**: Haem iron, the most bioavailable form
- **Lentils (dal) with lemon juice**: The vitamin C in lemon enhances non-haem iron absorption

Avoid tea, coffee, and milk alongside iron-rich meals — they reduce absorption.

## When to Seek Evaluation

See a gynaecologist if:

- You are meeting the "heavy" criteria above
- You suspect or have confirmed anaemia
- Bleeding is significantly affecting your daily life, work, or social activities
- You have not had a pelvic ultrasound in the context of these symptoms — fibroids and polyps are common in women in their 40s and can co-exist with perimenopausal changes
- Bleeding has become irregular in addition to heavy (spotting between periods, completely unpredictable timing)

A thorough evaluation should include pelvic ultrasound, thyroid function, full blood count, ferritin, and hormone levels.

## Management Options

A range of options exists to reduce heavy bleeding during perimenopause. A gynaecologist familiar with perimenopausal management can discuss which approaches are appropriate for your specific situation, including the causes identified in your evaluation.

The main point: you do not have to simply endure years of heavy bleeding because "perimenopause is just like this." Effective management exists.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does heavy bleeding during perimenopause last?**
Heavy perimenopausal bleeding typically begins in the early 40s for Indian women and can last 2 to 5 or more years, often peaking in the 2 to 4 years before the final menstrual period. It usually eases as cycles become infrequent in late perimenopause — but this can take many years, and management is available if bleeding is affecting your health or quality of life.

**Is heavy bleeding every month normal in perimenopause?**
Heavy bleeding in perimenopause is common — not all perimenopausal women experience it, but it is the experience of a significant proportion. "Common" does not mean it has to be endured without support. If it is affecting your iron levels, daily functioning, or quality of life, a gynaecologist should be consulted.

**Can heavy periods in perimenopause cause anaemia?**
Yes, and this is very common. Month after month of significant blood loss depletes iron stores, leading to iron-deficiency anaemia. Symptoms include extreme fatigue, breathlessness, brain fog, hair loss, and pallor. A serum ferritin test identifies this even before haemoglobin drops significantly. Treatment of anaemia alongside management of the bleeding is important.

**Do periods always get lighter before stopping in perimenopause?**
Not necessarily. Some women have increasingly heavy periods right up to their last period; others see a gradual lightening as cycles space out. The most typical pattern is: earlier perimenopause has heavier, more frequent periods; later perimenopause has periods that are further apart and eventually lighter; then cessation.

**What if my heavy periods are caused by fibroids, not just perimenopause?**
Fibroids and perimenopause often co-occur in women in their 40s and can compound each other's effects on bleeding. A pelvic ultrasound will identify fibroids. Treatment decisions depend on fibroid size, location, and your specific symptom picture — a gynaecologist should evaluate and discuss options.
