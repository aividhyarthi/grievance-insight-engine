**Key takeaways**
- Hair thinning and increased shedding during perimenopause are caused primarily by falling oestrogen and relatively higher levels of androgens (male-type hormones)
- The most common pattern is diffuse thinning — a wider parting, less volume overall — rather than bald patches
- Thyroid changes and iron deficiency (both common in perimenopause) can worsen hair loss and are worth checking

---

More hair on the shower floor. A wider parting. Ponytail thinner than it used to be. Hair loss during perimenopause affects a significant number of women and is one of the more distressing symptoms — because it is visible in a way that many others are not.

The good news: it is hormonal, it is largely reversible, and there are things that help.

---

## Why does perimenopause cause hair loss?

**Oestrogen is hair's best friend.** It extends the growth phase of the hair cycle, keeping each strand in the growing phase for longer before it sheds. When oestrogen levels fall — as they do in perimenopause — hair cycles shorten. More hairs enter the shedding phase at the same time, and regrowth slows.

At the same time, **androgens** (male-type hormones, including testosterone and DHT) become relatively more dominant as oestrogen and progesterone decline. Androgens shrink hair follicles — the same mechanism behind male pattern baldness, but in a milder, diffuse form in women.

The combined effect: more shedding, less regrowth, finer individual strands.

---

## What does perimenopausal hair loss look like?

Unlike male pattern baldness, perimenopausal hair loss is usually:

- **Diffuse** — thinning across the whole scalp rather than a specific bald area
- Most noticeable at the **parting** — it looks wider
- **More shedding** than usual — on the pillow, in the brush, in the shower
- **Finer strands** — individual hairs feel lighter and break more easily
- Sometimes **at the temples and hairline** as well as the crown

It is rarely dramatic baldness. But it is noticeable and, for many women, deeply upsetting.

---

## What else could be causing it?

In perimenopause, two other conditions can cause or worsen hair loss:

**Thyroid dysfunction** — the thyroid is directly affected by hormonal changes in perimenopause. Both underactive (hypothyroid) and overactive (hyperthyroid) thyroid cause hair shedding. If your hair loss feels sudden or severe, ask your doctor to check your thyroid.

**Iron deficiency** — heavy periods during perimenopause commonly cause iron levels to drop. Iron is essential for hair growth. Low ferritin (stored iron) is one of the most common and most frequently missed causes of hair loss in women.

It is worth getting both checked before assuming the hair loss is purely hormonal.

---

## What actually helps?

**Address underlying causes first.** If thyroid or iron levels are off, treating those may resolve a significant portion of the hair loss.

**Scalp care.** Gentle handling — less heat styling, softer brushes, loose hairstyles — reduces breakage and visible thinning. What you are losing is bad enough without mechanical damage adding to it.

**Protein and iron in your diet.** Hair is protein. Dal, paneer, eggs, and leafy greens all support hair growth. Low protein diets accelerate shedding.

**Reduce stress.** Chronic stress raises cortisol, which compounds hormonal hair thinning. Easier said than done — but worth acknowledging.

**Speak to a dermatologist or gynaecologist.** If hair loss is significant or accelerating, a proper assessment is worth it. There are effective treatments available for hormonal hair thinning. You do not have to just watch it happen.

---

## Will it grow back?

For most women, yes — especially if the root hormonal cause is addressed. Hair growth is slow (about 1 cm per month) so improvements take time to show. Do not judge any treatment in less than 3–6 months.

After menopause, when oestrogen stabilises at a lower but consistent level, many women find the shedding reduces significantly.

## FAQ

### Why is my hair thinning in my 40s?

Falling oestrogen and relatively dominant androgens during perimenopause shorten the hair growth cycle and shrink follicles. It is one of the most common — and most under-discussed — perimenopause symptoms.

### Is perimenopausal hair loss permanent?

It is usually not permanent. Hair loss tied to hormonal fluctuation tends to improve once levels stabilise. Addressing contributing factors like iron and thyroid helps significantly.

### Can perimenopause cause hair loss at the temples?

Yes. Androgenic hair thinning — which becomes more common in perimenopause — can affect the hairline, temples, and crown. It is different from alopecia areata (patchy loss) and usually responds to hormonal management.

### What tests should I ask for if I am losing hair?

Ask your doctor to check: thyroid function (TSH, T3, T4), ferritin (stored iron), and full blood count. These are the most commonly missed contributors to hair loss in women in their 40s.

### How long does it take for hair to recover after perimenopause?

Hair growth cycles are slow. Even after effective treatment, visible improvement takes 3–6 months. Be patient — and track progress by looking at new growth near the scalp rather than overall volume, which changes slowly.
