**Key takeaways**
- Yes — perimenopause commonly causes water retention through fluctuating oestrogen and falling progesterone levels
- It shows up as bloating, puffiness in the face and hands, swollen ankles, and a feeling of sudden heaviness
- Reducing salt, staying well hydrated, and gentle movement help — and the retention often improves once hormone levels stabilise

---

That puffy, heavy, bloated feeling in your 40s — even when you have not changed what you eat — is not your imagination. Water retention during perimenopause is very real, very common, and driven by your hormones.

---

## How does perimenopause cause water retention?

Two hormones are responsible.

**Oestrogen** affects how the body regulates fluid. When oestrogen fluctuates — surging and dropping unpredictably as it does in perimenopause — it causes the kidneys to retain more sodium. Where sodium goes, water follows. The result is fluid building up in your tissues.

**Progesterone** normally acts as a natural diuretic — it helps the body shed excess fluid. In early perimenopause, progesterone falls before oestrogen does. With less progesterone keeping fluid in check, oestrogen's fluid-retaining effects go unopposed.

The combination of high or fluctuating oestrogen and low progesterone is a common recipe for water retention.

---

## What does perimenopausal water retention feel like?

It is different from ordinary bloating after a heavy meal. You might notice:

- **Puffiness in your face** first thing in the morning
- **Swollen fingers** — rings feel tight
- **Swollen ankles or feet** by evening
- **Abdominal bloating** — clothes feel tighter without any weight gain
- A **general heavy, uncomfortable feeling** in your body

It often comes and goes with your cycle — worse in the second half, before your period, and better once bleeding starts.

---

## Is it water retention or actual weight gain?

A simple check: **does it fluctuate day to day or week to week?** True water retention can add 1–3 kg overnight and disappear just as quickly. Fat gain does not move like that.

If your weight swings noticeably within a few days and you notice puffiness rather than a consistent change in body fat, it is almost certainly hormonal fluid retention.

That said, perimenopause does also cause shifts in where the body stores fat — particularly to the abdomen. Both can happen at the same time. [Read more about weight changes in perimenopause](/guide/symptoms).

## What actually helps?

**Drink more water, not less.** Counterintuitive, but when the body is dehydrated it holds on to fluid. Staying well hydrated signals the kidneys to release excess fluid.

**Reduce salt.** Sodium is the main driver of fluid retention. Processed foods, pickles, papadums, and salty snacks are the biggest sources. Even a few days of lower salt intake makes a visible difference for many women.

**Move regularly.** Gentle movement — walking, yoga, swimming — helps fluid circulate and drain from tissues. Sitting or standing for long periods makes retention worse.

**Reduce alcohol and caffeine.** Both disrupt fluid balance and worsen bloating.

**Track your cycle.** If retention is cyclical and predictable, you can prepare for it rather than be surprised by it each time.

---

## When to speak to a doctor

Most perimenopausal water retention is manageable. But speak to your doctor if:

- Swelling is **sudden, severe, or on one side only**
- You have **shortness of breath or chest tightness** with swelling
- Swelling does **not improve** at all with lifestyle changes
- You have a history of heart, kidney, or liver conditions

These are unusual but need ruling out.

## FAQ

### Why am I retaining water all of a sudden in my 40s?

Fluctuating oestrogen and falling progesterone during perimenopause directly affect how your body regulates fluid. This is one of the most common — and least talked about — perimenopausal symptoms.

### Does water retention get worse before a period in perimenopause?

Yes. Retention is typically worst in the second half of the cycle (luteal phase) when progesterone would normally be rising but does not rise as much during perimenopause. Once your period starts, fluid usually releases.

### Will perimenopausal water retention go away on its own?

For most women, it improves as hormone levels stabilise — either with treatment or naturally after menopause. In the meantime, dietary and lifestyle changes can significantly reduce symptoms.

### Is bloating the same as water retention?

They overlap but are not identical. Bloating can also come from gut changes and gas. Perimenopausal women often experience both — gut motility is also affected by hormonal changes.

### Can I take anything for water retention during perimenopause?

Lifestyle changes (salt, water, movement) are the first step. Speak to your gynaecologist if symptoms are severe — there are options available that address the hormonal root cause rather than just the fluid.
