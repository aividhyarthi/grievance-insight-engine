You're 24, and you've had two missed periods in a row. You're having what feels like hot flashes — sudden waves of heat, night sweats that soak your clothes. Your mood is unpredictable in a way it never has been before. Your doctor orders a pregnancy test. Negative. She refers you to a gynaecologist, who looks surprised by your age.

The word "perimenopause" rarely comes up for a woman in her 20s. But the broader category — early ovarian changes — absolutely can affect women in their 20s, and knowing what it is and what to do matters enormously.

## Is "Perimenopause" the Right Word for Your 20s?

Technically, perimenopause refers to the years leading up to the natural menopause that most women experience in their late 40s to early 50s. For a woman in her 20s, the correct medical term for what may be happening is **premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)**, sometimes still called premature ovarian failure, though that older term is misleading.

POI is defined as loss of normal ovarian function before the age of 40. It affects approximately:
- 1 in 1000 women under the age of 30
- 1 in 100 women under the age of 40

If you are in your 20s with irregular or absent periods, hormonal symptoms, and elevated FSH on blood tests, POI is the diagnosis that needs to be evaluated — not typical perimenopause.

## What Is Premature Ovarian Insufficiency?

POI is not the same as typical menopause. The ovaries do not simply shut down. In most cases of POI, the ovaries still function intermittently — many women with POI still have occasional periods, and approximately 5 to 10% still achieve spontaneous pregnancy.

What happens in POI is that ovarian function becomes erratic and unreliable. The ovaries respond poorly to FSH signals from the brain. Hormone levels (oestrogen in particular) fluctuate and overall decline. The result is the symptoms associated with menopause: hot flashes, night sweats, irregular or absent periods, vaginal dryness, mood changes, sleep disruption, and brain fog — occurring in a woman far younger than expected.

## What Causes POI in the 20s?

In many cases, no clear cause is found. Where a cause is identified, common ones include:

- **Genetic conditions**: Fragile X syndrome (or fragile X premutation), Turner syndrome, and other chromosomal variations
- **Autoimmune conditions**: The immune system mistakenly attacks the ovarian tissue; often associated with other autoimmune conditions like thyroid disease or Addison's disease
- **Cancer treatment**: Chemotherapy and radiotherapy, particularly pelvic radiation, can damage the ovarian follicles
- **Surgery**: Removal of ovarian tissue or procedures that reduce blood supply to the ovaries

A thorough workup includes chromosomal testing, autoimmune antibody panels, and a detailed history.

## What Are the Symptoms to Watch For?

Symptoms that warrant prompt evaluation in your 20s include:

- Periods stopping or becoming very irregular (fewer than 4 per year)
- Hot flashes or night sweats
- Difficulty conceiving after 6 months of trying under age 35
- Vaginal dryness or discomfort during sex
- Mood changes, anxiety, or depression that feel hormone-related
- Fatigue, brain fog, and sleep disruption

A single missed period is not cause for alarm — stress, weight changes, and many other factors can disrupt cycles. But ongoing cycle irregularity alongside hormonal symptoms should be evaluated promptly.

## Why Early Diagnosis Matters So Much

For a woman in her 20s, POI is not just about fertility — though that may be a significant concern. The long-term health implications require attention:

**Bone health**: Oestrogen is critical for maintaining bone density. Women with POI who do not receive hormonal support have a significantly elevated risk of osteoporosis and fracture. Bone density testing (DEXA scan) is recommended.

**Cardiovascular health**: Oestrogen has a protective effect on the cardiovascular system. Early loss of oestrogen increases long-term cardiovascular risk if left untreated.

**Mental health**: The psychological impact of a POI diagnosis in your 20s — grief about fertility, adjusting to a changed sense of the future, managing symptoms that feel wildly out of place — is significant and deserves professional support.

Hormonal support (usually hormone replacement therapy) is recommended for most women with POI until the average age of natural menopause, primarily to protect bone and cardiovascular health — not just to manage symptoms. This is different from the nuanced risk-benefit discussion for older women taking HRT.

## What To Do If You're in Your 20s With These Symptoms

1. **See a gynaecologist promptly**: Do not wait months to see if things resolve. Cycle irregularity with hormonal symptoms in your 20s needs evaluation.

2. **Ask for specific blood tests**: FSH (measured twice, at least 4 weeks apart), LH, oestradiol, thyroid function, prolactin, and anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH). An ultrasound to assess antral follicle count is also useful.

3. **Ask about chromosomal testing**: Particularly fragile X premutation testing if POI is confirmed, as this has implications for your siblings and for your own neurological health.

4. **Discuss bone health**: Request baseline bone density testing if POI is confirmed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you have perimenopause at 22?**
The term "perimenopause" technically refers to the natural transition before menopause that typically occurs in the 40s and 50s. What happens in a woman's 20s is called premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) — a condition where the ovaries function inconsistently before age 40. POI can cause the same symptoms as perimenopause (irregular periods, hot flashes, mood changes) and needs prompt medical evaluation.

**What does premature ovarian insufficiency mean for fertility?**
POI does not mean zero chance of pregnancy. The ovaries still function intermittently in most cases, and approximately 5 to 10% of women with POI conceive spontaneously. However, fertility is significantly reduced and becomes unpredictable. A fertility specialist can discuss options including assisted conception.

**Is POI the same as early menopause?**
Not exactly. Early menopause means the ovaries have permanently stopped functioning before age 45. POI is characterised by intermittent ovarian function — it may not be permanent, and some women see a partial recovery of ovarian activity. However, POI often does lead to early menopause over time.

**Do I need hormones if I have POI at 25?**
For most women with POI, hormonal support is strongly recommended until the average age of natural menopause (around 51) — not just to manage symptoms, but to protect bone density and cardiovascular health. Discuss this with your gynaecologist. The risk-benefit calculation for young women with POI is different from that for older women.
