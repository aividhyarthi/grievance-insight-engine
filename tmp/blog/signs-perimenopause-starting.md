You know something has changed. You just cannot quite put your finger on it. Your mood is different — or your sleep is different — or your periods are different. Something in your body has shifted, but nothing is wrong enough to be a diagnosis, and you keep being told everything is fine.

This is perimenopause, in its earliest phase. And the reason it takes so long to name is that its first signs arrive separately, often years before your cycles become visibly irregular. Knowing what to look for changes everything.

## What Are the Very First Signs of Perimenopause?

### 1. Your Cycle Gets Slightly Shorter

Before any other obvious change, many women notice their menstrual cycle subtly shortens. Instead of a 28 to 30-day cycle, you might find 24 to 26-day cycles appearing — periods arriving a few days earlier than expected, month after month.

This happens because the luteal phase (the second half of the cycle, after ovulation) shortens as progesterone production becomes less robust. You are still ovulating, still cycling, still getting a period — but the hormonal architecture underlying it is starting to change.

Most women do not notice a 2-day shift in cycle length without tracking. If you track your cycle on an app or diary, look back: has it been shortening over the past year?

### 2. PMS Gets Noticeably Worse

If your premenstrual symptoms have escalated from manageable to something that affects your relationships, your ability to work, or your sense of yourself, this is a meaningful early signal.

Worsening PMS — more intense irritability, deeper low mood, greater anxiety in the days before your period — reflects the erratic progesterone production of early perimenopause. Progesterone's metabolite allopregnanolone acts on the brain's GABA receptors, providing a natural calming effect. As progesterone becomes less reliable, this buffer erodes.

Some women who had almost no PMS in their 30s develop severe premenstrual symptoms for the first time in their early 40s. Others find symptoms they managed for years become suddenly unmanageable.

### 3. Sleep Becomes Unreliable — Particularly in the Early Hours

Waking at 2am or 3am, unable to return to sleep despite feeling tired. This pattern — early morning waking rather than trouble falling asleep — is characteristic of low progesterone.

You may not be sweating. You may not be hot. You may simply be awake, mind active, lying in the dark for an hour or two. This is a hormonal symptom before it is a sleep disorder, and it is often one of the first things to improve when progesterone levels are supported.

### 4. A New Kind of Anxiety Appears

Not the general anxiety of having a lot to manage — but a physiological feeling. A sudden surge of unease. A sense of dread without clear cause. Difficulty settling the mind. Sometimes a racing heart or a feeling of panic that comes unpredictably.

This perimenopausal anxiety is tied to oestrogen's role in regulating serotonin, GABA, and the autonomic nervous system. As oestrogen begins to fluctuate erratically, the nervous system becomes less stable. The anxiety often correlates with the phase of the menstrual cycle — worse in the luteal phase (the two weeks before the period) before easing when bleeding begins.

In India, this symptom is overwhelmingly attributed to "stress" or "family pressure." While external stress is genuinely relevant, hormonal instability as a driver is almost never discussed.

### 5. Breast Tenderness Intensifies

Breast tenderness before the period that has become more noticeable, more painful, or starts earlier in the cycle is a common early perimenopausal signal. It is caused by oestrogen surges — and during perimenopause, oestrogen does not simply decline. It fluctuates erratically, with spikes that can be higher than pre-perimenopausal levels, stimulating breast tissue.

If breast changes are significant, new, or not clearly cyclic, see your doctor to rule out other causes — but cyclical breast tenderness in this context is very likely hormonal.

### 6. Brain Fog and Word-Finding Difficulties

Forgetting words mid-sentence. Losing a thought you had 30 seconds ago. Feeling mentally slower than you used to. This cognitive haziness often appears in early perimenopause, sometimes years before cycles become irregular.

Oestrogen receptors are present throughout the brain. Oestrogen supports memory consolidation, verbal fluency, and processing speed. As its levels begin to fluctuate unpredictably, these functions are affected.

This symptom resolves for most women after menopause, when hormone levels stabilise. But during the perimenopausal transition, it can feel alarming — particularly for women whose cognitive performance is central to their professional identity.

### 7. Periods Become Heavier

Heavy periods in the early 40s are often the most tangible and disruptive first signal — and one of the most medically important.

As anovulatory cycles increase (cycles where no egg is released), progesterone is absent, oestrogen builds the uterine lining unchecked, and the eventual shed is heavy and sometimes clotty. If your periods are noticeably heavier than they were in your 30s, this change is worth documenting and discussing.

## What Perimenopause Does NOT Look Like at the Start

Many women delay seeking help because they are waiting for the "classic" symptom: a dramatic hot flash. Hot flashes are more typical of mid-to-late perimenopause and menopause. In the early phase, the symptoms above are far more common — and far more often dismissed or misattributed.

You do not need to be having irregular periods to be in early perimenopause. You do not need to have hot flashes. You do not need to be in your late 40s.

## How to Know for Certain

Perimenopause is primarily a clinical diagnosis — made based on symptoms, age, and cycle history, not on a blood test alone.

Blood tests (FSH, LH, oestradiol) can support the picture, but hormone levels fluctuate so widely during perimenopause that a single normal result does not rule it out. Some women have clearly perimenopausal symptoms with completely "normal" blood results.

The most useful thing you can do is track your symptoms — their timing in relation to your cycle, their intensity, and their pattern over several months. Bring this record to a gynaecologist and ask specifically whether perimenopause could be a contributing factor.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the first sign of perimenopause for most women?**
For most women, the first sign is a change in the menstrual cycle — often the cycle shortening slightly, periods becoming heavier, or PMS worsening. Sleep disruption (particularly early morning waking) and new anxiety are also very early signs that often appear before hot flashes or irregular cycles.

**How early can signs of perimenopause start?**
For Indian women, early signs can appear in the late 30s. The average menopause age in India is 46 to 47, and the perimenopausal transition typically begins 4 to 10 years before the final period. Symptoms beginning at 38 or 40 are entirely plausible.

**Can you be in perimenopause with regular periods?**
Yes. Many women experience significant perimenopausal symptoms for years while still having fairly regular cycles. The cycle may shorten slightly, periods may become heavier, but the regularity can persist into early perimenopause. Irregular cycles typically appear later in the transition.

**Does perimenopause always start with hot flashes?**
No. Hot flashes are more typical of mid-to-late perimenopause and menopause itself. The earlier signs are usually more subtle: cycle changes, worsened PMS, sleep disruption, mood changes, and brain fog. Many women are in perimenopause for years before their first hot flash.

**What should I do if I think perimenopause is starting?**
Start tracking your symptoms — their timing, intensity, and relationship to your menstrual cycle. See a gynaecologist who takes a thorough hormonal history. Ask specifically about perimenopause.
